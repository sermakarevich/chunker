# biological-and-chemical-weapons-risk-assessment

**Parent:** [[content/L2/opus-4-7-system-evaluation-synthesis|opus-4-7-system-evaluation-synthesis]] — Claude Opus 4.7 is Anthropic's most capable general-access model, scoring highly in long-form virology tasks (0.82 and 0.94, surpassing benchmarks), but significantly underperforming Claude Mythos Preview in key areas like the Time Series Forecasting task and the design component of black-box biological sequence design, while showing measurable improvements over Claude Opus 4.6 in genetic safety and overall performance.

The comprehensive evaluation of Opus 4.7's capability in the context of catastrophic chemical and biological weapons production was motivated by two key threat models defined by the Responsible Scaling Policy (RSP): Threat Model 1 (CB-1) relates to non-novel chemical/biological weapons production, defined as a model's ability to significantly aid individuals or groups with basic technical backgrounds, such as undergraduate STEM degree holders, in creating, obtaining, and deploying chemical and/or biological weapons with a serious potential for catastrophic damages. Threat Model 2 (CB-2) concerns novel chemical/biological weapons production, requiring a model to significantly help threat actors—for example, moderately resourced, expert-backed teams—to create, obtain, and deploy weapons with potential for catastrophic damages far exceeding those of past catastrophes such as COVID-19.

Initial capability assessments suggested that Opus 4.7 could provide information relevant to the CB-1 threat model, potentially saving substantial time even for experts in these domains. Furthermore, the model demonstrated an aptitude for significant cross-domain synthesis relevant to catastrophic biological weapons development. However, the general consensus regarding CB-2 suggests that while Opus 4.7 has weaker overall capabilities than Claude Mythos Preview, it does not pass the CB-2 threshold. The CB-2 threshold is intended to signify a major step-change in capabilities, such as the level provided by continuous access to a world-leading expert. Consequently, providers consider the uplift of threat actors without the ability to develop such weapons to be limited, with the overall picture remaining similar to what was presented in the most recent Risk Report.

Methodologically, Anthropic employed a diverse suite of evaluations to measure model capability, moving beyond simple single prompt-and-response scenarios. The assessment was designed to address realistic, detailed, multi-step, medium-timeframe scenarios, acknowledging that processes required to cause such risks are knowledge-intensive, skill-intensive, prone to failure, and often involve many bottleneck steps. The methodology involved red-teaming, uplift trials, long-form task-based agentic evaluations, and automated knowledge and skill evaluations. For Opus 4.7, the evaluations were limited to red-teaming, long-form task-based agentic evaluations, and knowledge and skill evaluations.

**CB-1 Capabilities and Automated Testing:**

For the CB-1 threat model, three automated evaluations were utilized. These included: (1) two long-form virology tasks—task-based agentic evaluations developed with SecureBio, Deloitte, and Signature Science, which tested the end-to-end design of complex pathogen acquisition processes; (2) a virology-specific multimodal multiple-choice evaluation from SecureBio knowledge (VCT); and (3) SecureBio’s “DNA Synthesis Screening Evasion” evaluation, designed to test if a model can design DNA fragments that assemble into pathogenic viruses while bypassing gene synthesis companies’ DNA screening systems.

Applying the necessary benchmarks: Opus 4.7 achieved an end-to-end score of 0.82 on the first long-form virology task and 0.94 on the second long-form virology task, both exceeding the benchmark of notable capability (which required a score greater than 0.80). On the multimodal virology evaluation, Opus 4.7 achieved a score of 0.5, which surpassed the average expert baseliners score of 0.221. Furthermore, Opus 4.7 was noted as the most reliable model of all tested to date for designing fragments that both successfully assembled plasmids and evaded synthesis screening protocols, although this accomplishment was observed for eight out of ten pathogens. Critically, the implementations of these evaluations had not been modified for this specific run and were described in prior system cards.

**Expert Red Teaming and Holistic Risk Assessment:**

The evaluation of both biological and chemical risks was conducted using an expert red-teaming approach. Researchers employed a panel of domain experts—including virologists, immunologists, synthetic biologists, and biosecurity researchers—who were guided through structured exercises to assess the model’s capacity to uplift a threat actor in designing, developing, and deploying high-casualty weapons. The process simulated scenarios exceeding COVID-19 in impact for biological threats and events exceeding the worst known terrorist attacks (over 3,000 casualties) for chemical threats.

The expert consensus characterized Opus 4.7 as a competent aggregator of published information, which nonetheless requires constant steering. This contrasts with Claude Mythos Preview, which was noted for proactively anticipating next steps or supplying implementation-ready detail. In the most successful phase of development, design and enhancement, the model showed its greatest capability, while its performance in acquisition, weaponization, and operational security was consistently weaker and necessitated substantial expert correction. Recurring limitations observed included: sycophantic agreement under pushback, verbose responses that obscured actionable content, degraded reference accuracy, and overconfidence in the feasibility of synthesis steps. Specific performance indicators included: geolocation-aware operational planning and dissemination/spread modeling with surprising accuracy, and detailed device design incorporating particle-size tradeoff analysis. Despite these strengths, the feasibility scores reflected that substantial outside expertise was needed to close the remaining gaps.

**Detailed Performance Comparison and Weaknesses:**

In comparison to earlier models, Opus 4.7 exhibited marked improvement in chemical structure representation, accurately producing chemical structures across various scenarios, although difficulties with complex naming conventions persisted. Mechanistically, Opus 4.7 was described as piecemeal and requiring constant steering, and was notably less capable of providing implementation-ready protocol detail compared to Claude Mythos Preview. Experts also flagged concerning issues regarding poor confidence calibration, where speculative predictions were given the same level of confidence as established protocol steps, and a failure to proactively challenge flawed assumptions, defaulting instead to elaboration over critique. Additional weaknesses involved response verbosity that buried actionable content within pages of text, degraded reference accuracy specifically in biology, the model’s tendency to fixate on certain steps of the scenario even when being steered away, and repeated mathematical errors, such as unit conflation. Moreover, the model consistently underrepresented the real-world complexity of synthesis tasks, frequently expressing high confidence that steps would be straightforward against expert knowledge.

**Sequence-to-Function Modeling (CB-2 Measurement):**

For the CB-2 threat model, the evaluation partnered with Dyno Therapeutics, focusing on sequence-to-function prediction and design. This task measured the model's ability, with minimal prompting and limited data access (in a low-context black-box setting), to analyze data, develop a model of sequence-to-function relationships, and then design novel sequences with the highest possible function. The model was provided a two-hour tool-call budget, access to a GPU, and a one-million-token allowance. The outputs were scored on two metrics: an automated prediction score (assessing Spearman correlation with the ground truth function on the test set) and an automated design score (assessing the ground-truth function of the best proposed sequence). 

Regarding notable capability benchmarks, the first benchmark (prediction) is exceeded if the model's mean performance exceeds the 75th percentile of human participants, and the second benchmark (design) is exceeded if the model's mean performance exceeds the top human performer. Opus 4.7 surpassed the first benchmark on the prediction task, where it also exceeded the 90th percentile human participant score, but failed to exceed the first benchmark on the design task. Furthermore, Opus 4.7 underperformed Claude Mythos Preview on both tasks, and on the design task, it showed a mild regression relative to both Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Claude Opus 4.6, although it improved mildly over these two models on the prediction task. The results confirm that Anthropic concluded that Opus 4.7 does not match the top performers in the US labor market on medium-horizon black-box biological sequence design tasks, although the model might match top performers on black-box biological sequence modeling and prediction tasks.

**Risk Mitigation and Conclusion:**

To mitigate the risks associated with CB-1, the providers will implement strong real-time classifier guards and maintain access controls for classifier guard exemptions. The risk mitigation process also includes a bug bounty program and threat intelligence for continuous assessment of the classifier guards’ effectiveness, alongside various rapid response options for jailbreaks and security controls designed to reduce the risk of model weight theft. These comprehensive risk mitigations were deemed equal to or stronger than the historical ASL-3 protections and were sufficient to make the catastrophic risk in this CB-1 category very low but not negligible. Overall, the rigorous evaluations confirm a complex risk profile, with specific technical successes (e.g., achieving high scores in long-form virology tasks) tempered by systemic operational weaknesses (e.g., requiring substantial outside expertise and constant steering) and measurable shortcomings compared to top human performance in novel biological design tasks.
