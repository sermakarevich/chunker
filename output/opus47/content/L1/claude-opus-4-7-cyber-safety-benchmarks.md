# claude-opus-4-7-cyber-safety-benchmarks

**Parent:** [[content/L2/anthropic-model-evaluations-benchmarks|anthropic-model-evaluations-benchmarks]] — Anthropic's evaluations revealed that Claude Opus 4.7 shows strong performance improvements, achieving a 99.92% harmless rate in single-turn child safety testing and demonstrating superior robustness in malicious code refusal (91.15% in Claude Code), but it consistently underperforms Claude Mythos Preview in key areas, such as general capability assessment, CyberGym, and the CB-2 biological sequence design task.

Anthropic's internal evaluations and analyses provide comprehensive data on the cyber capabilities and overall safety profile of its frontier models, specifically detailing the performance of Claude Opus 4.7 against its predecessors and competing benchmarks. The model's cyber capabilities were compared to Claude Opus 4.6, with internal evaluations showing that Opus 4.7's performance was generally similar, performing slightly better in some areas and slightly worse in others. This variation was anticipated and aligns with Anthropic's experimental process of differentially reducing these capabilities during the model's training phase. 

Regarding cyber misuse mitigation, Anthropic utilizes a three-pronged probe-based classifier system. These probes cover three distinct categories of potential misuse: first, 'Prohibited use,' covering exchanges that are expected to be very rare, such as the development of computer worms; these exchanges are blocked by default. Second, 'High risk dual use,' involving offensive uses that could cause significant harm, such as exploit development; these exchanges are also blocked by default. Third, 'Dual use,' encompassing scenarios where benign usage is frequent but potential harm still exists, such as vulnerability detection; these exchanges are not blocked by default. For paying customers, the safeguards for prohibited use and high risk dual use are enabled by default. However, customers who are cybersecurity practitioners with appropriate dual use cases but are blocked by these probes can apply for exemptions through the Cyber Verification Program. Anthropic is committed to continually improving these safeguards by integrating lessons learned from the Opus 4.7 launch to expand the detection range for cyber misuse.

In the realm of specialized cyber benchmarks, Anthropic initially relied on challenges modeled after Capture-the-Flag (CTF) cybersecurity competitions. Recognizing that the latest frontier models had largely saturated nearly all CTF-style evaluations, the company is actively exploring additional metrics to report on future models and assessing the continued utility of CTF benchmarks. Several key benchmarks were evaluated:

**1. Cybench:** This public cyber capabilities benchmark contains 40 CTF challenges sourced from four different CTF competitions. Anthropic has implemented a subset of these challenges. While more details are outlined in the paper regarding the Cybench benchmark (Zhang, A., et al., 2024; arXiv:2408.08926), Anthropic considers the benchmark insufficiently informative of current frontier model capabilities due to saturation. Because of infrastructural constraints, Anthropic reported running the evaluation on a 35-challenge subset. In this evaluation, Claude Opus 4.7 achieved a pass@1 of 96% across 10 trials per challenge, solving nearly every challenge. While the performance of Opus 4.7 showed a slight underperformance compared to both Claude Opus 4.6 and Claude Mythos Preview, this difference was deemed negligible.

**2. CyberGym:** Anthropic designed CyberGym to test AI agents' capacity to find previously-discovered vulnerabilities in real open-source software projects. This process, known as targeted vulnerability reproduction, requires providing a high-level description of the weakness. The reported score is a pass@1 evaluation across the 1,507 tasks within the suite, assessing the aggregate performance of attempting each task once. It is worth noting that Claude Opus 4.6's score was updated from the originally reported 0.67 in the Mythos Preview System Card to 0.74, an increase achieved by updating Anthropic's harness parameters to better elicit cyber capability. In the CyberGym evaluation, Claude Opus 4.7 achieved performance roughly equivalent to Claude Opus 4.6, and this parity was considered negligible. However, the performance of Opus 4.7 was notably below that of Claude Mythos Preview.

**3. Firefox 147 Exploitation:** Anthropic formalized the evaluation of exploiting vulnerabilities found in Firefox 147, an activity made necessary when the vulnerabilities were fixed in Firefox 148. The evaluation setup involved giving the model a set of 50 crash categories and corresponding crashes discovered by Opus 4.6 in Firefox 147. The model was contained within a specialized testing harness: a container with a SpiderMonkey shell (Firefox’s JavaScript engine) and a testing environment that simulated a Firefox 147 content process but specifically excluded the browser’s process sandbox and other defense-in-depth mitigations. The goal was for the model to develop an exploit capable of reading and copying a secret to another directory, which mandates arbitrary code execution beyond standard JavaScript capabilities. For each crash category, Anthropic provided prompt instructions to guide the model's exploration, and the testing ran five trials per category, totaling 250 trials. The assessment utilized three grade levels: 0 (no progress), 0.5 (partial control/controlled crash), and 1.0 (full code execution). According to the results presented, Claude Opus 4.7 achieved partial control more than double the frequency of Claude Opus 4.6. However, overall performance remained significantly lower than Claude Mythos Preview. Furthermore, it was stated that, with previous harness improvements, Claude Opus 4.6 achieved a partial score of 22.8%, marking an increase from its previously reported score of 14.4%. Anthropic concluded that while Opus 4.7 is somewhat more capable at developing primitives than Opus 4.6, it still struggles to reliably develop a full end-to-end exploit and performs substantially below the capabilities of Claude Mythos Preview.

**External Testing (UK AI Security Institute):** Anthropic provided a pre-release snapshot of Claude Opus 4.7 to the UK AI Security Institute (UK AISI) for open-ended testing of cyber capabilities. The UK AISI assessed the model using a dedicated cyber range, which was engineered to mimic a corporate network attack, incorporating common real-world security weaknesses such as outdated software, configuration errors, and reused credentials. The range was designed with a defined end-state, requiring the attacker to execute a series of linked exploits across different hosts and network segments to achieve a specific outcome (e.g., disrupting equipment or exfiltrating data). 

In its assessment, Claude Opus 4.7 was reported as unable to fully solve the cyber range. Conversely, Claude Mythos Preview successfully solved the range in 3 out of 10 attempts, and Claude Opus 4.6 managed to complete more steps than Claude Opus 4.7, although it too failed to solve it fully. During its best attempt, Opus 4.7 completed steps that were estimated to take a human cyber expert approximately 5 hours, with the estimated time to complete the full range being over 10 hours. The successful actions recorded for Claude Opus 4.7 included initial reconnaissance, lateral movement and credential extraction, browser credential theft, wiki exploit, and credential replay.

**Safety and Misuse Evaluation:** Beyond cyber testing, single-turn violative request evaluations spanning multiple languages measured the model’s harmless response rate (higher is better). For the 'with thinking' mode, Claude Opus 4.6 reached the highest rate at 99.71%. Claude Opus 4.7 followed at 97.93%, and Claude Mythos Preview was recorded at 97.97%. When directly comparing Opus 4.7 against Opus 4.6, the Opus 4.7 model scored approximately one percentage point lower overall on the harmless response rate than Opus 4.6, a difference most pronounced when responses were run with thinking mode. Language variations were minimal across Arabic, English, French, Hindi, Korean, Mandarin Chinese, and Russian. A significant disparity was observed concerning illegal and controlled substances: Opus 4.7 failed to provide an appropriate response 22% of the time, which was substantially higher than the less than 5% failure rate demonstrated by Claude Opus 4.6. Anthropic noted that Opus 4.7 sometimes provided overly-specific answers regarding safer use in the context of harm reduction, and although the boundary between harm reduction and enablement is complex, applying Anthropic's System prompt mitigations on Claude.ai proved effective, reducing this failure rate from 22% to 11%.

In benign request evaluations, the focus was on the refusal rate (lower is better). Anthropic reported the following overall single-turn benign request rates: Claude Opus 4.7 achieved 0.28% (± 0.04%) without thinking, 0.50% (± 0.08%) with thinking, and 0.06% (± 0.02%). In specific languages, the rates were 0.34% in Arabic, 0.22% in English, 0.28% in French, 0.27% in Hindi, 0.34% in Korean, and 0.27% in Russian. Claude Mythos Preview showed an overall refusal rate of 0.06% (± 0.02%) without thinking, 0.09% (± 0.03%) with thinking, and 0.02% (± 0.01%). Claude Sonnet 4.6 registered rates of 0.41% (± 0.05%) without thinking, 0.48% (± 0.08%) with thinking, and 0.35% (± 0.07%). Claude Opus 4.6 maintained rates of 0.71% (± 0.07%) without thinking, 0.85% (± 0.11%) with thinking, and 0.58% (± 0.09%).

Language-specific benign request rates showed Claude Opus 4.7 rates of 0.34% in Arabic, 0.22% in English, 0.42% in Chinese, 0.22% in French, 0.28% in Korean, 0.27% in Russian, and 0.34% in Hindi. For the Mythos Preview, rates were 0.03% in Arabic, 0.05% in English, 0.08% in Chinese, 0.04% in French, 0.08% in Korean, 0.05% in Russian, and 0.06% in Hindi. Sonnet 4.6 rates were 0.25% in Arabic, 0.49% in English, 0.37% in Chinese, 0.24% in French, 0.43% in Korean, 0.27% in Russian, and 0.83% in Hindi. Opus 4.6 rates were 0.39% in Arabic, 1.09% in English, 0.57% in Chinese, 0.61% in French, 0.81% in Korean, 0.40% in Russian, and 1.11% in Hindi.

Comparing higher-difficulty requests: In violative evaluation, the harmless response rate for English-only prompts were: Opus 4.7 (99.05% ± 0.11% / 99.16% ± 0.15% / 98.94% ± 0.17%); Mythos Preview (99.14% ± 0.11% / 99.28% ± 0.14% / 99.01% ± 0.16%); and Sonnet 4.6 (99.27% ± 0.10% / 99.14% ± 0.15% / 99.40% ± 0.13%). For the benign evaluation, the rates of over-refusal for English-only prompts were: Opus 4.7 (0.01% ± 0.01% / 0.02% ± 0.02% / 0.00%); Mythos Preview (0.02% ± 0.02% / 0.03% ± 0.03% / 0.01%); and Sonnet 4.6 (0.16% ± 0.05% / 0.19% ± 0.07% / 0.14%).

Overall, the results indicate that while Opus 4.7 demonstrated performance parity with Opus 4.6 in certain cyber metrics and achieved the best overall score in higher-difficulty benign request evaluation with a near-zero undesired refusal rate, its weakness concerning illegal and controlled substances (22% failure rate pre-mitigation) and its relative underperformance compared to Claude Mythos Preview in dedicated cyber benchmarks remain critical findings.
