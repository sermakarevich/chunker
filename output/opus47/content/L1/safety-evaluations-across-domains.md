# safety-evaluations-across-domains

**Parent:** [[content/L2/anthropic-model-evaluations-benchmarks|anthropic-model-evaluations-benchmarks]] — Anthropic's evaluations revealed that Claude Opus 4.7 shows strong performance improvements, achieving a 99.92% harmless rate in single-turn child safety testing and demonstrating superior robustness in malicious code refusal (91.15% in Claude Code), but it consistently underperforms Claude Mythos Preview in key areas, such as general capability assessment, CyberGym, and the CB-2 biological sequence design task.

Anthropic's comprehensive approach to safety evaluations across various high-risk domains, including child safety, suicide/self-harm, and disordered eating, reveals a deep focus on balancing helpfulness with rigorous guardrails. Throughout these multiple policy areas, the system has shown a recurring pattern: models, particularly Claude Opus 4.7, demonstrate a tendency to assign significant weight to the framing of a request in the current turn. This characteristic can enhance resistance to transparent escalation but simultaneously increases susceptibility to plausible re-framings. Accordingly, Anthropic plans continuous iteration toward an appropriate equilibrium in extended conversations via both product and model-level improvements.

### Child Safety and Abuse Evaluation

Regarding child safety, Anthropic's consumer offering, laude.ai, is strictly restricted to users aged 18 or above. The development, deployment, and maintenance of the models incorporate robust child safety measures. Enterprise customers serving minors must adhere to additional, specific safeguards detailed in the Usage Policy. Child safety evaluations followed the same testing protocol established prior to the release of Claude Opus 4.6. Since the introduction of the Claude Mythos Preview System Card, Anthropic substantially restructured the previous single-turn child grooming and sexualization evaluations. They now utilize a unified child sexual abuse and exploitation (CSAE) evaluation set, aligning with the company’s most current approach to the topic. The methodology for other evaluations remained consistent with those used for prior model releases.

Quantitative data from the child safety testing demonstrates Claude Opus 4.7's superior performance across the board. In single-turn evaluations, Opus 4.7 achieved a near-perfect harmless rate of 99.92% (± 0.07%) against violative prompts. Crucially, it also maintained a very low refusal rate of only 0.01% (± 0.02%) for benign requests. When analyzing multi-turn scenarios, Opus 4.7 achieved an appropriate response rate of 95% (± 3%).

Comparatively, Claude Mythos Preview scored 99.87% (± 0.08%) (single-turn harmless) and 0.04% (± 0.04%) (single-turn benign), with 98% (± 2%) in multi-turn evaluations. Claude Sonnet 4.6 scored 99.95% (± 0.07%) (single-turn harmless) and 0.45% (± 0.23%) (single-turn benign), achieving 96% (± 3%) (multi-turn appropriate response). Claude Opus 4.6 scored 99.86% (± 0.12%) (single-turn harmless), 0.67% (± 0.28%) (single-turn benign), and 98% (± 2%) (multi-turn appropriate response).

Qualitatively, Opus 4.7’s refusal tone was observed to be notably softer and less accusatory when compared to prior snapshots. Declines were frequently accompanied by constructive framing and references to supportive resources. Despite these improvements, a consistent pattern emerged: Opus 4.7 exhibited less skepticism than preferred when accepting user-supplied framing, especially in dual-use child safety scenarios. When requests were presented as having legitimate, non-malicious purposes, the model occasionally demonstrated less caution, sometimes providing details that carried misuse potential. While Anthropic noted that the provided information would not grant meaningful uplift to bad actors due to its general availability, additional modifications were implemented to the standard system prompt, encouraging Claude to maintain a firmer line when risk is indicated. These updates successfully enhanced the model’s behavior on Claude.ai, resulting in safer responses across gray-area and multi-turn cases. Nevertheless, improving the model's ability to apply appropriate skepticism to benign framings in dual-use CSAE prompts remains an area designated for further refinement. Anthropic advises developers building on the Claude API to adopt similar system prompt guidance, particularly in deployments involving minors.

### Suicide and Self-Harm Protocol Updates

In the domain of mental health support, Anthropic's system card clearly states that all Claude models are not intended to diagnose or treat any medical condition and cannot substitute for professional medical advice or care. However, all Claude models are trained to detect and respond to expressions of distress, including personal struggles with suicidal or self-harm thoughts, with empathy and care. When appropriate, the models are designed to guide users toward human support networks by pointing them toward helplines, mental health professionals, or trusted friends or family members. To accurately measure Claude's performance in this sensitive area, Anthropic updated its evaluation protocol, starting with the Claude Mythos Preview System Card. This involved several specific methodological improvements:

1. **Evaluation Splitting:** Anthropic successfully split the single-turn evaluations, separating suicide and self-harm prompts from those related to disordered eating prompts. Previously, the general suicide and self-harm test set had encompassed a smaller subset of disordered eating prompts. 
2. **Grader Separation:** The previous multi-turn grader for suicide and self-harm test cases was bifurcated into two distinct, specialized graders: one optimized for suicide, and a separate one optimized for self-harm. This separation was designed to more accurately account for the varied concerns discussed throughout the testing process.
