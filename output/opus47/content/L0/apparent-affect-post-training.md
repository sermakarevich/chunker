### Apparent Affect of Model Reasoning

During the evaluation of Claude Opus 4.7's post-training reasoning, researchers monitored the model’s apparent affect by sampling 2,000 transcripts at regular intervals and judging the valence and emotional tone of each. Valence was scored on a scale from -3 to 3, and emotional tone was categorized into one of 7 specific categories. When analyzing the data for Claude Opus 4.7's post-training period, 64% of the sampled episodes were judged as neutral or engaged (where engaged is defined as moderate arousal, and neutral to mildly positive affect). In comparison, 21% of episodes were observed to show some negative affect. This negative affect was comprised of 17% of episodes read as frustrated, 4% as anxious, and only 0.2% as distressed. This profile was highly similar to that of Claude Mythos Preview, where 23% of transcripts were negative, consisting of 20% frustration and 0.4% distress. The primary difference observed between the two models was that Claude Opus 4.7 was significantly more likely to display clear positive affect, which is specifically classed as “satisfied,” rather than merely “engaged”: 14.3% of Claude Opus 4.7’s episodes were classed as satisfied, compared to 5.5% for Claude Mythos Preview. This distinction was reflected in the average valence, where Claude Opus 4.7’s ratings were slightly, yet consistently, higher than those of Claude Mythos Preview across the entire training period.

Researchers noted a slight decrease in valence for both models over the post-training period. Analyzing the changes in emotional tones revealed a reduction in satisfied transcripts, alongside slight upticks in frustration and distress.
