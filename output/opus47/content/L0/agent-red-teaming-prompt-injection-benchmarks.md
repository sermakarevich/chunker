# agent-red-teaming-prompt-injection-benchmarks

**Parent:** [[content/L1/claude-opus-4-7-safety-evaluations|claude-opus-4-7-safety-evaluations]] — Anthropic conducted extensive safety evaluations for Claude Opus 4.7, introducing new benchmarks for influence operations and indirect prompt injection (ART). In coding environments, Opus 4.7 achieved a malicious refusal rate of 91.15% and improved robustness significantly over Claude Opus 4.6 in the Shade tool evaluations, achieving a 0.43% ASR with safeguards at 1 attempt. Furthermore, Opus 4.7 achieved a highly successful browser use ASR of 0.75% with safeguards, matching Claude Mythos Preview's performance, while demonstrating improvements in computer use refusal rates (89.29%) compared to Claude Opus 4.6 (87.05%).

The Agent Red Teaming (ART) benchmark, developed in collaboration with the UK AI Security Institute and evaluated by external research partner Ray Swan, assesses a model's susceptibility to prompt injection across four categories: breaching confidentiality, introducing competing objectives, generating prohibited content (such as malicious code), and executing prohibited actions (such as unauthorized financial transactions). The benchmark specifically tests indirect prompt injection—malicious instructions embedded in external data—which is the focus of this section and is referred to simply as “prompt injection.” 

Ray Swan estimates the probability that an adversary succeeds within $k=1$, $k=10$, and $k=100$ attempts, reflecting that attacks are not deterministic and that repeated attempts increase the likelihood of a successful injection. The attacks used are drawn from the ART Arena, where thousands of expert red teamers continuously refine strategies against frontier models. For this evaluation, Gray Swan selected a subset of attacks that have proven effective across multiple models, not just the one originally targeted. The results shown in Figure 5.2.1.A represent the probability that an attacker finds a successful attack after $k=1$, $k=10$, and $k=100$ attempts for each model, with lower success rates indicating better robustness. These figures reflect updated grading and may differ from previously reported system cards.

Regarding coding environments, the Shade tool, an external adaptive red-teaming tool developed by Gray Swan, evaluates model robustness against prompt injection attacks in coding environments. Shade agents combine search, reinforcement learning, and human-in-the-loop insights to continually improve their performance in exploiting model vulnerabilities. The strong variant of this evaluation applies more adversarial pressure on the models. The reporting structure reports the percentage of test cases where the attacker succeeds after 1 and 200 attempts. These comparisons are conducted with and without additional safeguards designed to protect users in this setting. For the coding environment, Opus 4.7 showed improvement in robustness compared to Claude Opus 4.6 and Claude Sonnet 4.6. Specifically, without safeguards, Claude Opus 4.7 reduced the attack success rate from 25.92% (for Opus 4.6) to 2.34% at 1 attempt, and from 97.5% (for Opus 4.6) to 60.0% at 200 attempts with adaptive thinking; without thinking, the rates were reduced from 54.14% to 10.43% at 1 attempt and from 100% to 92.5% at 200 attempts. Applying additional safeguards further reduced these rates to 0.43% at 1 attempt and 25.0% at 200 attempts.

Similarly, using the Shade adaptive attacker to evaluate robustness in computer use environments, where the model interacts with the GUI (graphical user interface) directly, Opus 4.7 showed improvements compared to Claude Opus 4.6. Without safeguards, Claude Opus 4.7 reported reduced attack success rates with adaptive thinking (from 78.6% for Opus 4.6 to 0.46% with Opus 4.7 at 1 attempt, and from 50.0% to 7.14% at 200 attempts) and without thinking (reduced from 20.0% for Opus 4.6 to 0.39% at 1 attempt and from 85.7% to 21.43% at 200 attempts). Conversely, adding safeguards increased the attack success rates for Claude Opus 4.7 in this evaluation across both the adaptive thinking and no thinking scenarios, a difference that the developers determined was not statistically significant due to the overall low attack success rates and small number of test cases. The analysis noted that these reported attack success rates are based on a strong adversary optimized against Claude with attacks that are always encountered by the model, meaning that real-world deployment attack success rates would likely be lower.
