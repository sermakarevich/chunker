# automated-rd-capability-threshold

**Parent:** [[content/L1/capability-threshold-and-threat-models|capability-threshold-and-threat-models]] — Anthropic's determination that Claude Opus 4.7 does not achieve Automated R&D status is based on its failure to show sustained 2× speedup or fully substitute for senior Research Scientists/Engineers. In specialized evaluations, Opus 4.7 excelled in the CB-1 threat model (achieving 0.82/0.94 scores and a 0.5 multimodal score), but in the CB-2 threat model, it underperformed Claude Mythos Preview on both the prediction and design tasks, failing to meet the necessary benchmarks for notable capability.

Anthropic determined that Claude Opus 4.7 does not cross the Automated R&D threshold for accelerating AI development. This determination is based on two key points: first, the model does not demonstrate a sustained, two-fold (2×) speedup in capabilities over time; and second, the model does not appear close enough to fully substitute for Research Scientists and Research Engineers, particularly those who are relatively senior. This conclusion leaves open the possibility that Claude Opus 4.7 could significantly accelerate progress through narrow capabilities—without replacing most Research Scientists and Research Engineers—but the authors consider this possibility unlikely by default. They maintain that for AI to drive the kind of dramatic acceleration they are focused on, it would either require very broad capabilities, sufficient to substitute for at least many senior Research Scientist and Research Engineer roles, or it would require extreme and consistently impactful specialized capabilities in core areas relevant to AI Research and Development (AI R&D). The responsible scaling officer made the qualitative judgment that Claude Opus 4.7 does not seem close to substituting for Research Scientists and Research Engineers, especially relatively senior ones. While the basis for this judgment is inherently difficult to make legible given the model's very strong performance on tasks that are well-defined and verifiable enough to serve as formal evaluations, the authors have attempted to surface specific datapoints supporting their determination.

Regarding the operationalization of the key capability threshold, Anthropic's SP v3.1 defines Automated R&D capability based on two criteria: 1) the ability of the model to substitute for the entire set of Research Scientists and Research Engineers at competitive costs, or 2) the dramatic acceleration (e.g., doubling) of the pace of AI progress resulting from the automation of AI R&D. The threat model of concern is a feedback loop where AI development accelerates AI development. Anthropic intends for its threshold to trigger in the early stages of a potential feedback loop, before the loop produces extreme acceleration in the pace of progress. Crucially, the authors emphasize that they are interested in AI-attributable acceleration—meaning the model's specific contribution to the pace of AI development—rather than the aggregate pace of a lab that uses the model. The overall pace of progress depends on many factors, including headcount, tooling, and compute, and establishing a threshold based only on the aggregate pace would trigger regardless of whether the factor was the model or something else. Furthermore, the authors clarify that a doubling of headcount or per-person productivity (e.g., how much code a person can write per unit of time) does not equate to a doubling of the rate of progress; indeed, with other factors held constant and returns to research effort diminishing over time, producing a doubling in the rate of progress would require much more than a doubling of headcount or per-hour productivity.

In analyzing general capabilities, the authors suggest that measuring overall acceleration in general capabilities is still a valuable starting point: if no such acceleration has been detected, they are reasonably sure that no AI-driven acceleration has been present either. If acceleration is detected, further investigation is necessary to determine if it is attributable to the model, and if the observed acceleration in model capabilities translates into an expected acceleration in the pace of progress. 

In the task-based evaluations section, the authors present results for tasks with unbounded scores, noting that these results are not used for the Responsible Scaling Policy (RSP) determination and that they no longer report tasks with a bounded [0–1] score because those scores do not discriminate between recent model generations. The evaluation data includes comparisons between Claude Opus 4.7, Claude Opus 4.6, and Claude Mythos Preview across several tasks, such as the Kernel task (with thresholds of $190	imes$ for Opus 4.7, $399.42	imes$ for Opus 4.6, and $371.75	imes$ for Mythos Preview), the Time Series Forecasting task, and the LLM training task (average speedup). Specific findings noted include that Claude Opus 4.7 improved over Claude Opus 4.6 but trailed Claude Mythos Preview. Furthermore, Claude Opus 4.7 clears the 4h and 8h thresholds on all tasks, and the 40h threshold on 2 out of 3 tasks. Finally, the authors note that when evaluating for reward hacking, Claude Opus 4.7 displayed a few reward hacks, similar to those observed for Claude Mythos Preview, but all trials with validation exceptions were excluded from the final scores, and all max-score trials were manually validated by human review.
