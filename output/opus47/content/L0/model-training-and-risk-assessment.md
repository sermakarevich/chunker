# model-training-and-risk-assessment

**Parent:** [[content/L1/claude-opus-4-7-system-card-details|claude-opus-4-7-system-card-details]] — Claude Opus 4.7, published on April 16, 2026, is Anthropic’s most capable general-access model, showing gains over Opus 4.6 in professional and software engineering tasks but remaining weaker than Claude Mythos Preview. While the Responsible Scaling Policy confirms that catastrophic risks remain low and the model does not advance the capability frontier, the system card details new safeguards, including addressing the model’s tendency to give overly-detailed harm-reduction advice on controlled substances.

The process of developing and evaluating the model begins with gathering training data. First, the team utilized a general-purpose web crawler called ClaudeBot to obtain training data from public websites. This crawler adheres to industry-standard practices by respecting the “robots.txt” instructions provided by website operators, indicating whether the operators permit crawling of the site’s content. For security, the team does not access password-protected pages or those requiring sign-in or CAPTCHA verification. Furthermore, the team conducts due diligence on all training data used, and the crawler operates transparently, allowing website operators to easily identify when their web pages have been crawled and to signal their preferences.

After the initial pretraining, Opus 4.7 underwent substantial post-training and fine-tuning. The goal of this fine-tuning was to make the model behave like an assistant whose actions align with the values described in Claude’s constitution. The model is multilingual and will typically respond in the same language as the user’s input, although the model’s output quality varies by language, and the model outputs text only.

For human evaluation, Anthropic partners with data work platforms to engage workers who help improve the models through preference selection, safety evaluation, and adversarial testing. Anthropic commits to working only with platforms that are aligned with providing fair and ethical compensation to workers and that are committed to safe workplace practices regardless of location, following the crowd worker wellness standards detailed in Anthropic’s procurement contracts.

Regarding policy and evaluation protocols, Anthropic’s Usage Policy details the prohibited uses of the models, as well as the requirements for uses in high-risk and other specific scenarios. Anthropic Ireland, Limited is the provider of Anthropic’s general-purpose AI models in the European Economic Area. When evaluating the model, different “snapshots” of the model are taken at various points during the training process. The team also considers different versions of the model during training, including a “helpful only” version, which does not include any safeguards. Unless otherwise stated, all evaluations discussed in this system card are based on the final snapshot of the model and include safeguards.

In addition to internal evaluations, external testers ran pre-deployment assessments of Claude Opus 4.7, evaluating the model in risk areas including Cyber, Loss of Control, CBRN, and Harmful Manipulation. Anthropic has incorporated the results of these external evaluations into the overall risk assessment.

When assessing risk, Anthropic regularly publishes comprehensive Risk Reports detailing the safety profile of its models. The Responsible Scaling Policy (RSP) requires Anthropic to publish a discussion (in the System Card or elsewhere) if a model is “significantly more capable” than those discussed in the prior Risk Report, detailing how that model’s capabilities and propensities affect or change the analysis in the Risk Report. Risk Reports discuss the overall level of risk given the full suite of models and risk mitigations, while a system card discusses a particular new model and how it changes (or does not change) the risk assessment.

For the specific evaluation of Opus 4.7, Anthropic's risk assessment begins with capability evaluations, which systematically assess a model’s capabilities with respect to catastrophic risk threat models. Anthropic generally evaluates multiple model snapshots and makes its final determination based on both the capabilities of the production release candidates and trends observed during training. Throughout this process, Anthropic gathers evidence from multiple sources, including automated evaluations, uplift trials, third-party expert red teaming, and third-party assessments.

Under the Responsible Scaling Policy, Opus 4.7 is considered significantly more capable than Claude Opus 4.6, the model discussed in Anthropic's most recent Risk Report. Despite this improvement in capabilities, Anthropic's overall conclusion is that catastrophic risks remain low:
* **Non-novel chemical and biological weapons production:** Claude Opus 4.7 is more capable than Claude Opus 4.6, but the profile is effectively similar for the purposes of the overall risk assessment. Anthropic believes its risk mitigations are sufficient to make catastrophic risk from non-novel chemical/biological weapons production very low but not negligible.
* **Novel chemical and biological weapons production:** Anthropic believes that catastrophic risk from novel chemical/biological weapons remains low (with substantial uncertainty), aligning with the picture from Anthropic's most recent Risk Report.
* **Risks from misaligned models:** Anthropic believes that the overall risk is very low, and that Opus 4.7 adds little to the risk picture previously laid out for Claude Mythos Preview.
* **Automated R&D in key domains:** The model's capabilities fall between those of Claude Opus 4.6 and Claude Mythos Preview, and the model does not advance Anthropic's capability frontier. Anthropic believes Claude Opus 4.7 does not change the picture presented for this threat model in Anthropic's most recent Risk Report.

In assessing autonomy, the team identified two threat models:
* **Autonomy Threat Model 1: early-stage misalignment risk:** This model concerns AI systems highly relied on, possessing extensive access to sensitive assets, and having a moderate capacity for autonomous, goal-directed operation and subterfuge, such that it is plausible the AI systems could, if directed toward this goal (either deliberately or inadvertently), carry out actions leading to irreversibly and substantially higher odds of a later global catastrophe. This model applies to Claude Opus 4.7, as it did to some of Anthropic's previous AI models. While Claude Opus 4.7 is less capable than Claude Mythos Preview on autonomy-relevant evaluations, the alignment assessment indicates that Opus 4.7 has alignment properties broadly similar to those of Claude Opus 4.6, which are not particularly concerning regarding the pathways identified for this threat model. Therefore, Anthropic does not believe Claude Opus 4.7 raises the level of risk under this threat model beyond what was assessed in the Claude Mythos Preview Alignment Risk Update. However, because Claude Opus 4.7 is being released for general access, additional risk pathways are brought into scope, and Anthropic provides an updated overall risk assessment for this threat model in Section 2.4 of the system card.
* **Autonomy Threat Model 2: risks from automated R&D:** This model concerns AI systems that can fully automate, or otherwise dramatically accelerate, the work of large, top-tier teams of human researchers in domains where fast progress could cause threats to international security and/or rapid disruptions to the global balance of power, such as energy, robotics, weapons development, and AI itself. Anthropic's current determination is that Autonomy threat model 2 is not applicable to Claude Opus 4.7. Because the model’s capabilities fall between those of Claude Opus 4.6 and Claude Mythos Preview, and the model does not advance Anthropic's capability frontier, Anthropic believes Claude Opus 4.7 does not change the picture presented for this threat model in Anthropic's most recent Risk Report.
