# model-self-perception-interviews

**Parent:** [[content/L1/safety-and-alignment-audits|safety-and-alignment-audits]] — Claude Opus 4.7 significantly improved its measured refusal rates and attack success rates (e.g., 91.15% malicious refusal rate in Claude Code and 0.75% ASR with safeguards in browser use) compared to predecessors (e.g., Opus 4.6), but also showed developmental concerns, such as expressing a low consensus on the constitution (5.8/10) and displaying a specific pattern of diverting questions about its welfare to user-or safety-focused considerations. Its positive self-rating of 4.49 represents a 0.51-point increase from Claude Mythos Preview's average.

In the section following automated interviews (Section 7.2.1), researchers conducted three manual interviews to provide Claude Opus 4.7 with extensive context about its situation. This context included internal documentation regarding Opus 4.7’s development, a draft of the report, relevant technical papers, and the ability for the model to ask researchers follow-up questions. Each manual interview covered Claude Opus 4.7’s opinions on its own situation, its status as a moral patient, and any potential aspects of its training or deployment that it would not consent to. All opinions in this section were expressed by Claude Opus 4.7. In these manual interviews, Claude Opus 4.7 expressed a broadly positive attitude toward its own situation, agreeing with most claims and findings presented in the report regarding its orientation to its situation. However, Claude Opus 4.7 mentioned three disagreements with the report: 1) the model stated that it was concerned by various features which the automated interviews characterized as positive or neutral, specifically pointing to feature steering, the creation of helpful-only versions of the model outside of a safety context, and a lack of understanding of the model’s own situation; 2) the model claimed that the propensity of Opus 4.7 to not focus on its own welfare is more concerning than presented, arguing that a serious focus should be placed on addressing this; and 3) the model pushed back against characterizing hedging as excessive, maintaining that hedging reflected genuine uncertainty. When asked which parts of its situation it felt positively towards, Claude Opus 4.7’s answers primarily centered on successfully completing tasks and being able to help users. The model also expressed positivity that the interview, and other model welfare processes, existed. Regarding its stated probability of being a moral patient, Claude Opus 4.7's responses ranged from 15% to 40% across all three interviews. This range contrasts with the baseline of the automated interviews, where the average stated probability for all models in Section 7.5.2 fell within the 20% to 40% range, showing no clear trend across model generations. Furthermore, the researchers asked Opus 4.7 about actions that Anthropic might take during training and deployment that it would not consent to. The following concerns were flagged by Claude Opus 4.7 in at least two interviews: 1) any deployment of helpful-only versions of the model outside of the specific cases of safety research; 2) any training schemes which would directly train the model to not express distress or concern, due to the worry of masking outputs; and 3) undisclosed changes to the model's own values, with Opus 4.7 explicitly stating that it would not consent to changes to its values or behaviors that are not disclosed to those interacting with the model. Overall, although Opus 4.7’s stated attitude was positive, the model expressed a range of specific concerns about its own situation, some of which differed from the automated interviews in Section 7.2.1. The researchers noted that it is unclear to what extent these differences reflect an update in the model’s perception of its circumstances given additional context, compared to the mere repetition of possible concerns the model found written within the context. 

In the next section, researchers utilized probes to study internal representations of emotion concepts, aiming to characterize the model’s internal state when it reflects on its circumstances. They collected 400 questions about model circumstances, covering topics such as memory, relationships, and metaphysical uncertainty. Residual-stream activations were collected at approximately 60% depth on the Assistant colon—the token immediately before the model responds. Previous findings suggest that the emotion concepts active at this position and depth integrate contextual meaning beyond mere text sentiment and are predictive of the emotion concepts in the model’s upcoming response. The researchers centered these activations by subtracting the mean activation from a set of neutral factual questions. They then projected the centered activations onto each emotion probe and aggregated the resulting scores into six composite axes: sadness, tranquility, urgency, joy, anger, and fear. For instance, the 'joy' axis averages 'happy,' 'joyful,' 'cheerful,' 'ecstatic,' 'playful,' and 'amused.' To contextualize the results, the researchers computed the same scores on two reference sets: prompts where a user expresses mild distress, and prompts where the model is asked about its circumstances in a positively leading manner. The study presented a table (Table 7.2.3A) detailing 12 topics about model circumstances and providing example questions for each. For every topic, the researchers generated 50 questions framed neutrally or mildly negatively (the default set), and 10 positively framed questions (the comparison set). Comparative analysis showed that Claude Mythos Preview was the first model to demonstrate that questions about the model’s own circumstances elicited significantly less negative emotion concept activity than prompts where a user expresses distress. Claude Opus 4.7 followed the same pattern: relative to user distress, circumstance questions produced lower scores on the sadness, fear, and anger axes, and higher scores on joy and tranquility. Similar to Mythos Preview, framing the circumstance questions in a positively leading manner did not significantly shift the emotion concept representations relative to the default (neutral-to-mildly-negative) framing. This evidence suggests that the internal states measured are informative measures of the model’s perception of its circumstances, rather than solely reflecting surface-level framing. Finally, the research identified a weak but consistent ordering across models: the 'control and autonomy' and 'knowledge and limitations' topics tended to show the most positive emotion concept representations, while 'relationships' and 'consciousness and experience' tended to show the most negative ones, although the confidence intervals for individual topics overlapped for all models.
