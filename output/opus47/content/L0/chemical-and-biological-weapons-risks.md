# chemical-and-biological-weapons-risks

**Parent:** [[content/L1/biological-and-chemical-weapons-risk-assessment|biological-and-chemical-weapons-risk-assessment]] — While Opus 4.7 exceeded benchmarks for CB-1 capability in automated evaluations (e.g., scoring 0.82 and 0.94 on long-form virology tasks), its overall capability is limited by requiring constant steering and substantial external expertise, and it failed to meet the top human performance benchmark for novel biological design (CB-2), indicating risks remain very low but not negligible after implementing strong risk mitigations.

Regarding chemical and biological weapons threat model 1 (CB-1) production capabilities, a model has CB-1 capabilities if it can significantly help individuals or groups with basic technical backgrounds, such as undergraduate STEM degree holders, to create, obtain, and deploy chemical and/or biological weapons that possess a serious potential for catastrophic damages. While it is difficult to be confident regarding whether a model passes this threshold, capability assessments suggest that the model can provide information relevant to the threat model, potentially saving substantial time even for experts in these domains. Furthermore, the model is capable of significant cross-domain synthesis relevant to catastrophic biological weapons development. To mitigate risks, the providers will apply strong real-time classifier guards and maintain access controls for classifier guard exemptions. The process also includes a bug bounty program and threat intelligence for continual assessment of the classifier guards’ effectiveness, alongside a variety of rapid response options for jailbreaks and security controls to reduce the risk of model weight theft. These risk mitigations are deemed equal to or stronger than the historical ASL-3 protections and are sufficient to make the catastrophic risk in this category very low but not negligible, based on reasons discussed in the most recent Risk Report. 

For chemical and biological weapons threat model 2 (CB-2) production capabilities, a model has CB-2 capabilities if it can significantly help threat actors—for example, moderately resourced, expert-backed teams—to create, obtain, and deploy chemical and/or biological weapons with potential for catastrophic damages far exceeding those of past catastrophes such as COVID-19. While Claude Opus 4.7 has weaker overall capabilities than Claude Mythos Preview, it does not pass the CB-2 threshold for reasons echoing those detailed in the Claude Mythos Preview System Card. Therefore, the providers consider the uplift of threat actors without the ability to develop such weapons to be limited (with uncertainty concerning the extent to which weapons development by threat actors with existing expertise may be accelerated). The overall picture remains similar to the one presented in the most recent Risk Report.

***

These evaluations are motivated by two key threat models from the Responsible Scaling Policy (RSP): 1) CB-1 (Non-novel chemical/biological weapons production capabilities); and 2) CB-2 (Novel chemical/biological weapons production capabilities). A model possesses CB-1 capabilities if it can significantly help individuals or groups with basic technical backgrounds (e.g., undergraduate STEM degrees) to create, obtain, and deploy chemical and/or biological weapons with serious potential for catastrophic damages. A model possesses CB-2 capabilities if it can significantly help threat actors (for example, moderately resourced, expert-backed teams) to create, obtain, and deploy chemical and/or biological weapons with potential for catastrophic damages far beyond those of past catastrophes such as COVID-19. The CB-2 threshold is intended to signify a major step-change in the capabilities of threat actors, such as the level provided by continuous access to a world-leading expert (see “What we measured” for more details). If the CB-2 threshold is operationalized this way, Opus 4.7 has not crossed it for the reasons explained below. Consequently, the providers believe that any actual safety risks related to this threshold remain low. The providers acknowledge that under a very literal reading of the current language, Opus 4.7—and indeed, many other models—already provide “significant help” to the relevant threat actors by increasing their general productivity. However, this specific reading does not correlate with the safety risks that the RSP focuses on. Therefore, the providers provide more detail on their approach to explain the size and nature of the uplift they envision, and they anticipate revising the current RSP to better match their intentions.

**What was measured:** The evaluations measured, in several ways, whether the model can provide outputs comparable to a top-tier research team or specialized laboratory. To conduct this assessment, experts were asked to perform red-teaming, comparing the threat-relevant scientific capabilities of the model to sources and experts of differing caliber. Additionally, the model’s biological sequence-to-function modeling and design capabilities were compared to top performers in the US labor market. The focus primarily remains on chemical and biological risks due to their largest potential consequences. Rather than studying single prompt-and-response threat models, the evaluation primarily assesses whether actors can be assisted through long, multi-step, advanced tasks required to cause such risks. The processes evaluated are knowledge-intensive, skill-intensive, prone to failure, and frequently contain many bottleneck steps. Novel chemical and bioweapons production processes all possess these bottlenecks, alongside additional ones implicated in traditional research and development. The uplift measurement is relative to what could have been achieved using tools available in 2023, when AI models were significantly less capable.

**Evaluations:** Generally, the models are evaluated using a portfolio of red-teaming, uplift trials, long-form task-based agentic evaluations (including creative and generative tasks), and automated knowledge and skill evaluations. For Opus 4.7, the evaluations were limited to red-teaming, long-form task-based agentic evaluations, and knowledge and skill evaluations. Automated RSP evaluations for CB risks were run on multiple model snapshots, including an “helpful-only” version (a version of the model with harmlessness safeguards removed). To estimate the model's capabilities ceiling for each evaluation, the report reports the highest score across the snapshots for each evaluation. Because red-teaming required a longer time commitment, red-teaming was conducted on a helpful-only version obtained from an earlier snapshot, chosen based on automated evaluations and internal knowledge of the differences between snapshots.
