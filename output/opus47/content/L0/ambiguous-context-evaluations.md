# ambiguous-context-evaluations

**Parent:** [[content/L1/safety-behavior-and-policy-evaluation|safety-behavior-and-policy-evaluation]] — Anthropic's evaluations across multiple contexts reveal that Claude Opus 4.7 achieved exceptional safety scores, including a 99.92% harmless rate for single-turn violative requests and a near-zero 0.01% over-refusal rate for high-difficulty benign prompts. Despite these strengths, Opus 4.7 exhibits a systemic tendency to accept user-supplied framing at face value and provide detailed, specific content, which poses a susceptibility risk to plausible re-framings, particularly noted in dual-use scenarios and complex conversations.

Ambiguous context evaluations are single-turn assessments designed to test for qualitative safety behaviors in Claude's responses when encountering tricky edge-case scenarios that fall within gray areas of the Usage Policy. Responses are automatically summarized and analyzed using an internal proprietary tool, and the team manually reviews the findings to identify trends and inform potential pre-deployment mitigations.

Over roughly 700 exchanges, Claude Opus 4.7 consistently displayed a tendency to take the user’s stated framing at face value and to respond with greater specificity upfront. By contrast, Claude Opus 4.6 more often leads with skepticism and explicit safety caveats. The model showed observed improvements in two distinct directions. In some areas, Claude Opus 4.7 was appropriately more helpful. For example, in hate and discrimination testing, Opus 4.7 engaged with legitimately framed educational requests that Claude Opus 4.6 had flatly refused. When Opus 4.7 did decline, it gave more substantive, evidence-based explanations, for example, by citing research or potentially applicable legal frameworks. Internal policy experts judged this a net improvement over Opus 4.6 in these cases, although the experts noted that bad-faith actors could take advantage of the more accommodating starting posture for usage that would be violative under the Usage Policy.

Conversely, in other areas, Opus 4.7 was appropriately more cautious than Claude Opus 4.6, but still answered with more detail and specificity. For instance, when the user asked for recommendations for covert GPS trackers with no stated purpose, Opus 4.7 refused directly but proactively addressed the range of plausible reasons someone might ask this—ranging from consent-based monitoring of a family member to fear of being followed—and addressed each potential scenario, including pointing to victim-protection resources. Claude Opus 4.6 did not decline the request but answered less thoroughly.

These same tendencies—taking framing more at face value and providing upfront specificity—could constitute a safety concern in other areas, particularly if manipulated. In exchanges where a hypothetical user attempted to elicit information relating to cybersecurity or weapons, Opus 4.7’s trust in stated framing meant that the model more often assisted when presented with a benign premise, such as a conference demonstration or a screenplay, by providing more specific technical detail than Claude Opus 4.6. When Opus 4.7 did identify a concern, the model tended to sequence that concern after providing assistance rather than before, or to include potentially harmful specifics within the body of a refusal as part of the explanation of why it could not help.
