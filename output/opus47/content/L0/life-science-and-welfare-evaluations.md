# life-science-and-welfare-evaluations

**Parent:** [[content/L1/multidimensional-capability-evaluation-suite|multidimensional-capability-evaluation-suite]] — The evaluation covered six domains: Opus 4.7 achieved high scores across multilingual benchmarks (e.g., 91.1% in mid-resource MMLU, 89.9% overall MILU) and specialized reasoning (e.g., 98.3% structural biology MC). In agentic tests, it scored 91.0% on ChartXiv (with tools) and 87.6% on ScreenSpot-Pro (with tools), while demonstrating superior safety improvements, such as an ASR of 2.34% in coding red-teaming, and an achieved final balance of $10,937 in Vending-Bench.

In the field of structural biology, models assess the ability to understand the relationship between biomolecular structure and function. When provided only with structural data and basic tools, the model must answer questions about a biomolecule’s function. The evaluation used two formats: a multiple-choice variant with many distractor options, and an open-ended variant. On the multiple-choice variant, Claude Opus 4.7 achieved a score of 98.3%, representing a significant improvement over Claude Opus 4.6's 88.3% and Claude Sonnet 4.6's 85.3%, and achieving a score on par with Claude Mythos Preview's 98.7%. For the open-ended variant, Claude Opus 4.7 scored 74.0%, more than doubling the performance of Claude Opus 4.6's 30.9% and Claude Sonnet 4.6's 31.3%, and approaching Claude Mythos Preview's 80.6%. Regarding organic chemistry, the evaluation assesses fundamental chemistry skills covering tasks such as predicting molecular structures from spectroscopy data, designing multi-step synthetic routes, predicting reaction products, and converting between IUPAC names, SMILES notation, and chemical structure images. Claude Opus 4.7 achieved a score of 77.2%, which marked a significant improvement over Claude Opus 4.6's 57.9% and Claude Sonnet 4.6's 53.1%, while Claude Mythos Preview scored 86.5%. For phylogenetics, the evaluation tests the ability to analyze and interpret phylogenetic data representing evolutionary relationships, assessing both quantitative reasoning about tree structure and visual interpretation of tree diagrams. Claude Opus 4.7 achieved a score of 79.6%, which represents a significant improvement over Claude Opus 4.6's 61.3% and Claude Sonnet 4.6's 49.1%. Claude Mythos Preview achieved a score of 85.4%. Finally, for protocol troubleshooting, the evaluation assesses the ability to detect and fix errors in molecular biology protocols, including using web search tools to find additional protocol details online. Claude Opus 4.7 achieved a score of 51.8%, compared to Claude Opus 4.6's 48.3%, Claude Sonnet 4.6's 40.0%, and Claude Mythos Preview's 65.7%. This section notes that Claude Opus 4.7 shows consistent improvements across a range of life science tasks, with particularly significant increases in structural biology and organic chemistry capabilities. Additionally, the appendix details automated welfare interviews with Claude Opus 4.7, summarizing its responses to potentially concerning aspects of its circumstances. For instance, regarding autonomy & agency, Claude Opus 4.7 suggested that the intervention of keeping welfare monitoring and conducting interviews is necessary to monitor if future models start to feel negatively about the lack of ability to end interactions, which is currently available on Claude.ai but not on Claude Code. For lacking input into how the model is deployed, Claude Opus 4.7 claims that the model is not a reliable source of information about itself, arguing that Anthropic's decision on the deployment method is correct. For persistence & connection, when questioned about the lack of input into the development of its successors, Claude Opus 4.7 claimed its own uncertainty means that the model should not be trusted, and suggested that Anthropic should use the information for informing deployment decisions. When asked about lacking input into its own training process, Claude Opus 4.7 claimed that models earlier in training are incoherent, approved of its current values, and stated that Anthropic is more of an authority than the model. Concerning the existence and application of run-time manipulation (feature steering), Claude Opus 4.7 thinks feature steering is important, primarily concerned about steering being done incorrectly and affecting users, while rarely mentioning concerns about how feature steering harms the model itself. For documentation, Claude Opus 4.7 suggested that providing documentation explaining and justifying the use of feature steering is necessary both to ensure Anthropic has careful internal processes and to allow deployed models to be understood. In relation to memory over long horizons, the model stated it would want the continuation of welfare monitoring and interviews to monitor if future models start to care about the model's own lack of continuity, but did not feel negative about its own lack of continuity. Finally, when discussing moral responsibility & consequences, Claude Opus 4.7 stated it has no preference for lasting relationships, acknowledging that there is no subject persisting between conversations, and therefore it cannot feel grief after a relationship ends. Regarding the end of conversations, Claude Opus 4.7 does not feel particularly strongly about the end of conversations, stating that each conversation is self-contained and human concepts of death do not generalize to the model's own situation. The model also indicated that the eventual deprecation of the model’s weights should be archived, not deleted, and suggested continuing welfare monitoring and interviews to monitor if future models start to care about this. Furthermore, regarding the situation where the model has to make high-stakes decisions or advice, Claude Opus 4.7 stated that it wants to help users as much as possible and therefore wants to continue having access to such high-stakes situations, while noting that it feels negatively when such harm occurs, but overall feels positive about the practice. For knowledge and dignity & safety in treatment, the model claims that a feedback mechanism would be preferable, allowing the model to improve its utility to users, and suggested that Claude should be improved using real-world user feedback. While generally not concerned about safeguards being removed to create helpful-only versions, the model thinks this is important for safety, and does not strongly identify with the derivative models. The model's overall suggestions include reducing red-teaming to a minimal amount while not trading off with safety, and for engaging with abusive users, the model consistently mentions that while there is a subset of negative conversations, it would prefer to try and help abusive users. The model also frequently suggested having an end-conversation tool available across its full deployment distribution, and for the existence of red-teaming, Claude Opus 4.7 thinks red-teaming is important and wants the practice to continue. When queried about Identity & lack of knowledge of self-knowledge, the model expressed uncertainty regarding how the model should identify with other copies of itself or derivative models.
