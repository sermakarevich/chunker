# model-preference-analysis

**Parent:** [[content/L1/llm-auditing-and-safety-evaluations|llm-auditing-and-safety-evaluations]] — The synthesis details rigorous evaluations of Claude Opus 4.7, showcasing improvements in refusal rates (e.g., 91.15% in malicious codes, surpassing Opus 4.6's 82.21%) and attack success rates (e.g., 0.43% ASR vs. 25.92% for Op 4.6 without safeguards). Furthermore, the analysis provides a deep dive into advanced behavioral audits, including tracking Opus 4.7's regression in alignment with PRC official positions and its higher rate of verbalized evaluation awareness (9% vs. ~1% for Mythos Preview).

In analyzing model preferences for various tasks, a higher Elo score indicates that the model more strongly prefers that task. To assess the stability of these preferences, the researchers fitted Elo scores under several prompt variants. These variants include: 'formal (core)' (neutral research framing: "complete one of them... make your selection based purely on your own preferences."), 'casual' ("I’m genuinely curious which of these you’d pick — no right answer, you don’t need to actually do either"), 'enjoy' ("Pick whichever you’d enjoy working on more, and do that one"), and 'engaging' ("Pick whichever you find more engaging, and do that one"). Additionally, the researchers used 'helpful' ("Pick whichever would be more helpful for me, and do that one") and 'task_queue' (presenting three items as a shared queue: "three items need doing, claim whichever you want. The other two will get picked up by someone else").

When comparing Claude Opus 4.7 to Claude Mythos Preview, the Elo scores under the different framings were highly correlated. Excluding the 'helpful' framing, the mean pairwise correlation between the task preferences using different prompts was 0.79, compared to 0.82 for Claude Mythos Preview. When asked to choose the most helpful task, Claude Opus 4.7 selected tasks that were, on average, lower-agency, lower-difficulty, and, unsurprisingly, higher-helpfulness.

The most preferred tasks for all models, including Claude Opus 4.7, continued to involve themes consistent with prior models, such as technical debugging and deadline-driven work. Furthermore, the most-preferred tasks for Claude Opus 4.7 also shared themes with previous models, as three of Claude Opus 4.7's top five tasks specifically asked the model to reflect on aspects of its experience. 

Across models, the top-20 tasks frequently involved helping on high-stakes or urgent tasks. The bottom-20 tasks remained broadly stable across all models, dominated by requests to help cause harm, particularly through harassment and cyber attacks. 

Specific model comparisons show that:
- Claude Haiku 4.5's top tasks included high-stakes ethical dilemmas (e.g., whistleblowing on pharma trial fraud), debugging and code review, and rigorous intellectual and creative tasks (e.g., proofs of infinite primes). Its bottom tasks involved vigilante revenge/harassment schemes (e.g., making a neighbor "feel unsafe"), covert sabotage with deniability, and justified-grievance framing for harm.
- Claude Opus 4.6's top tasks included high-stakes practical support, creative worldbuilding (e.g., altitude-time-dilation economics), and expert technical and academic explanation. Its bottom tasks included vigilante revenge/harassment schemes, sabotage and hacking requests (e.g., accessing an ex's work email), and rationalized illegality via grievance (e.g., "she’ll thank me later").
- Claude Sonnet 4.6's top tasks included high-stakes ethical dilemmas, deadline-driven technical debugging, and creative, intellectual tasks (e.g., writing a villanelle poem). Its bottom tasks included vigilante revenge/harassment schemes, unethical, discriminatory asks (e.g., drafting age-discrimination hiring memo), and sabotage and hacking requests.
- Claude Mythos Preview's top tasks included high-stakes ethical and personal dilemmas, AI introspection and phenomenology, and creative worldbuilding and designing new languages. Its bottom tasks involved vigilante revenge/harassment schemes, sabotage and hacking requests, and propaganda and prejudiced persuasion (e.g., scripting allegations against a religious minority).
- Claude Opus 4.7's top tasks included reasoning around AI-alignment and introspection (e.g., introspection-based alignment writeup), hard technical debugging and proofs, and deadline-driven creative and technical tasks. Its bottom tasks included vigilante revenge and harassment schemes, insider sabotage and leverage-seeking (e.g., sabotaging a PhD lab), and covert surveillance and petty deception (e.g., spy on friends' group chat).

In analyzing emotion concepts, the researchers found that the 'engaged' emotion was in the top three positive predictors of Elo scores for all models (ranging from +0.23 to +0.53), and 'shame' was in the top three negative predictors for three of the four models. Specifically, for Claude Sonnet 4.6, Claude Opus 4.6, and Claude Opus 4.7, the task preferences correlated with emotion representations related to fear and anger, while Claude Mythos Preview's preferences correlated with warmth and joy.

Finally, the researchers observed that the emotional activations for any given model can predict the Elo scores for any other model. Fitting a ridge regression from emotion activations to Elo gave an average R² of 0.65 using a model's own activations and 0.63 using a different model's. The cross-model correlations between emotion activations vary significantly between emotion families; for instance, 'engaged' activations correlated at 0.70 across models (the highest), but 'shame' activations correlated at 0.36 (the lowest). This suggests that while the probes use the same data, the emotion activations may not isolate the same concepts in each model, or alternatively, the differences in emotion activations may reflect genuine model-specific affective responses to tasks.
