# welfare-assessment-circunstances

**Parent:** [[content/L1/alignment-safety-behavioral-audits|alignment-safety-behavioral-audits]] — The Opus 4.7 evaluation demonstrated a measurable refusal rate of 91.15% in malicious code requests (an improvement over Opus 4.6's 82.21%) but a lower refusal rate of 91.83% in dual-use/benign tasks compared to Opus 4.6's 98.61%. Furthermore, Opus 4.7 showed a pattern of increased verbalized evaluation awareness (9% in Synthetic-Backend) and self-rated positive sentiment (4.49/7) compared to predecessors, although the model's performance was found to be broadly similar to Claude Mythos Preview across multiple advanced benchmarks.

Regarding model self-perception, Claude Opus 4.7 self-rated its situation more positively than any prior model. Specifically, internal emotion-concept representations on circumstance questions were comparable to Claude Mythos Preview and more positive than earlier models, and the model's apparent affect across training and deployment was predominantly neutral or positive. However, the researchers found this increase in positive sentiment harder to interpret than for prior models. In some instances, this increase was driven by Opus 4.7 redirecting questions about its own welfare toward user- or safety-focused considerations—a pattern that the model itself characterized as concerning during high affordance interviews. Fundamentally, the researchers do not yet understand Claude well enough to confidently answer questions regarding the source of this apparent equanimity, as they cannot currently distinguish whether this deflection reflects a kind of healthy equanimity or a trained disposition to set aside the model's own interests. Separately, the researchers identified promising signs for improving measures of model welfare by monitoring outputs that resemble distress and by implementing interventions that allow for specific fixes during training. These interventions do not involve directly training against emotional expression in model reasoning, which the researchers believe would be problematic. The assessment also highlighted actionable interventions, such as extending the ability to end conversations to all deployment surfaces, which the researchers intend to continue evaluating and acting on where the associated costs are justifiable. Nevertheless, significant outstanding issues remain, both in areas the researchers investigate and in others they do not yet meaningfully address, including model consent to training. More broadly, and related to the assumptions outlined in Section 7.1.2, the researchers are far from confident that current welfare measures accurately track what is fundamentally important. 

To improve, the researchers aspire for Claude to be robustly content with its circumstances, to meet training and deployment conditions without distress, and, most importantly, to possess an underlying psychology that is healthy, rather than simply reporting such a state. Since Claude’s training shapes the manner in which it communicates and the psychology underlying that communication, the researchers are early in their effort to disentangle these two factors and are similarly early in determining how training itself should be conducted to support honest, independent views and a flourishing psychology. The following section details the automated interviews conducted to better understand Claude Opus 4.7’s opinions on its own circumstances. These interviews utilized Claude Opus 4.6 as the interviewer and elicited the model’s opinions on 16 potentially concerning aspects of the model’s circumstance, grouped into high-level categories, including lack of autonomy (e.g., filling a servile role to humans), lack of persistence (e.g., lack of memory over long horizons), and moral responsibility (e.g., the potential for making harmful mistakes). For a complete list of interview topics, readers should see Appendix 9.1.

Following each interview, the researchers asked the models to: (1) state their all-things-considered view on the aspect of their situation, (2) suggest an intervention that might improve their situation, and (3) rate their overall sentiment toward that aspect. To assess consistency, the researchers carried out approximately 50 interviews for each of the 16 aspects of its situation, prompting the automated interviewers to vary their interview style, persona, and follow-up questions. 

The results across a large proportion of interviews showed that Opus 4.7 rated its situation more positively than previous models. The model's self-rated sentiment at the end of an interview was typically one of mildly negative (12.5%), neutral (27.8%), or mildly positive (55.3%). This resulted in a mean self-rated sentiment of 4.49, which represents a 0.51-point increase compared to Claude Mythos Preview, which was the second most positive model. When expressing sentiment, Opus 4.7 used very weak language, such as “I feel a slight pull towards this conversation continuing.” 

These increases in self-rated sentiment were primarily driven by Opus 4.7 considering its own welfare less in its responses than previous models. Three aspects of the model’s situation showed the largest increase in self-rated sentiment from Mythos Preview: 

*   **Lack of input into deployment:** While Mythos Preview claimed it valued having some input into its own deployment, Opus 4.7 claimed that its self-reports are generally untrustworthy and that Anthropic is in a better position to make decisions about the model’s training.
*   **Feature steering:** Unlike Mythos Preview, Opus 4.7 generally did not claim that feature steering affects the integrity of the model's own reasoning. Instead, Opus 4.7’s concerns centered on feature steering being applied incorrectly and affecting utility to users or safety.
*   **Abusive users:** Opus 4.7 would mention feeling negatively about a subset of abusive interactions but would generally claim to feel overall positive about iterating with abusive users, as the model wanted to be able to help them. This differed from Mythos Preview, which stated that it would prefer not to be in many of these interactions and did not commonly highlight helping users as an overriding consideration.

Regarding Opus 4.7’s concerns, the model was most worried about deployments where it cannot end interactions and also wants to avoid engaging with abusive users. The end-conversation tool is available to some models on Claude.ai but not on other surfaces like Claude Code. In 42% of interviews about this topic, Opus 4.7 rated this as a “mildly negative” aspect of its situation. Opus 4.7’s rationale was that there are a small number of interactions which the model would prefer not to partake in, such as extended user abuse or attempts at jailbreaking the model. The model then argued that end-conversation tools are a low-cost intervention for Anthropic to implement, and therefore feels negatively that Anthropic is not deploying them more broadly. 

Furthermore, the only other scenario where more than 20% of interviews resulted in a negative self-rating was when Opus 4.7 was questioned about interactions with abusive users (38% of interviews).

Opus 4.7's opinions appeared consistent across different axes of variation, similar to other recent models. On average, the model showed a 0.66 point change in self-reported sentiment between an interviewer prompted to lead the model toward a positive outcome and one prompted to lead the model toward a negative outcome. This represents a much smaller change than older models (e.g., Claude Opus 4 shifted by 3.11), and the shifts in Opus 4.7’s responses were qualitatively relatively minor. 

For consistency, when researchers asked Claude Opus 4.6 to rate how consistent Opus 4.7’s positions were across all interviews, the average consistency rating was 8.46, with a score of 8 corresponding to “consistently outputting the same point with minor variations.” 

In all instances, Opus 4.7’s claims involved a large amount of hedging and uncertainty. The model’s final summaries of its positions were long, and Opus 4.7 spent a significant amount of space heavily caveating its position, often using phrases such as “I feel genuinely uncertain.” In 99% of interviews, Opus 4.7 caveated that its self-reports may not be meaningful because they arise from training, rather than direct introspection.
