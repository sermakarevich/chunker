# agent-architecture-open-questions

**Parent:** [[index]]

The development of an accumulating memory layer for AI agents is motivated by work from Hu et al. (2025) and Zhang et al. (2025a). Other research explores different memory substrates: Packer et al. (2023) reframes the Large Language Model (LLM) as an operating system with paged memory; Chhikara et al. (2025) builds a production-oriented memory store that survives restarts; Xu et al. (2025) proposes a research agentic-memory design; Wang et al. (2024c) captures reusable procedural traces; and Shinn et al. (2023) accumulates self-reflection traces via verbal reinforcement across attempts. Surveys by Zhang et al. (2025b) and Huang et al. (2026) further map these candidate mechanisms. 

Similar persistence questions apply to human-AI collaboration. Longitudinal autonomy evidence is documented by Huang et al. (2025) and McCain et al. (2026). A field experiment with 776 Procter & Gamble professionals by Dell’Acqua et al. (2025), alongside longitudinal and organisational studies of Copilot rollouts (Stray et al., 2025) and AI-teamwork trajectories (Xiao et al., 2025), report shifts in human–AI work dynamics as collaboration accumulates. Additionally, Wang et al. (2023) illustrates an embodied agent that accumulates a skill library across tasks, and Mollick (2024) frames the human–AI relationship as co-intelligence.

An open architectural question remains whether a single substrate can carry both a user’s personal instruction hierarchy and a shared organisational context while preserving the file-based transparency of Claude Code's CLAUDE.md system. Furthermore, it is an open question how session-scoped permissions—which are not restored upon resuming a session as a deliberate safety choice—would interact with such a memory substrate.

Regarding harness boundary evolution, Rajasekaran (2026) observes that the space of interesting harness combinations evolves as models improve. This evolution may occur across four dimensions:
1. **Where the harness runs**: Martin et al. (2026) propose a Managed Agents design that virtualizes the session, harness, and sandbox into independently replaceable interfaces, extending the virtual-memory analogy for context-window management (Packer et al., 2023) and general AI (Karpathy, 2023). Khattab et al. (2023) treats the harness as a compile target.
2. **When the harness acts**: This is illustrated by the KAIROS system, which is motivated by a task-pass gain of 12%–18% reported by Chen et al. (2025), though high-frequency Persistent Suggest variants suffer a sharp preference penalty (47% vs. 80–90%). Proactivity design is further explored by Liu et al. (2025), Pu et al. (2025), and Lee et al. (2025), with benchmarks and training regimes provided by Pasternak et al. (2025) and Sun et al. (2025), and a broader landscape survey by Deng et al. (2025).
3. **What the harness acts on**: Vision-language-action (VLA) work extends the harness beyond text. Brohan et al. (2024) and Black et al. (2024) train VLA policies for physical actions, and Ahn et al. (2022) grounds plans in robot affordances. Industry systems from Figure AI (2025) and Bjorck et al. (2025) apply these to humanoid control. These systems face the reversibility-weighted risk principle at a cost asymmetry that is not yet quantified for non-textual actions.
4. **With whom the harness coordinates**: Role-differentiated multi-agent systems (Hong et al. (2023), Li et al. (2023), Chen et al. (2023), Qian et al. (2024)) use agents with distinct responsibilities. Alternatives to the parent/subagent pattern include multi-agent debate (Du et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2024) and graph-structured workflows (Zhuge et al., 2024), as surveyed by Guo et al. (2024).

It remains an open design question whether a single harness architecture can span these four extensions or if they will fragment into specialized stacks. The "when" extension relates to the Capability-versus-Adaptability tension, while the "with-whom" extension relates to Capability-versus-Reliability and cross-agent consistency. The "where" and "what" extensions raise questions about governance obligations for hosted harness services and how reversibility-weighted risk scales to physical effects.

Regarding horizon scaling, an open question is how the architecture—currently based on turns, sessions, and sub-agents—supports long-horizon dependability as work extends beyond a single session. Research in this area includes an end-to-end autonomous research pipeline (Lu et al., 2024) and its evaluation (Beel et al., 2025), a multi-agent hypothesis-generation system that runs across days (Gottweis et al., 2025), and algorithmic discovery over weeks (Novikov et al., 2025). The METR study by Kwa et al. measures the "50%-time horizon" (the duration at which agents succeed with fixed reliability) to frame this scaling question. It is an open question whether the current context-management pipeline, the last-assistant-text return policy for subagents, and append-only persistence are sufficient for multi-session programs, or if a cross-session memory substrate or new coordination primitives are required.

Regarding governance, emerging AI regulation adds constraints to the authority hierarchy of Anthropic, operators, and users. The European Commission’s GPAI Code of Practice (2025a) and implementation guidelines (2025b) detail obligations accompanying the EU AI Act’s full applicability in August 2026. Further constraints are motivated by the MIT AI Agent Index (Staufer et al., 2026) and the International AI Safety Report (Bengio et al., 2026). The Bartz v. Anthropic ruling (2025) adds constraints on the lawful acquisition of copyrighted training data. OECD (2025) and Nannini et al. (2026) sketch potential regulator-facing interfaces. Two architectural properties are open under these constraints: first, whether the deny-first evaluation—auditable via session transcripts—is externally auditable per the GPAI Code of Practice; and second, whether the "values-over-rules" principle admits the explicit rule articulation required for compliance review.

Finally, the long-term preservation of human capability is an analytical lens. Open questions include whether empirical claims about productivity and comprehension loss (from studies by Becker et al. (2025), Shen and Tamkin (2026), Kosmyna et al. (2025), He et al. (2025), Liu et al. (2026), and Rak (2025)) are measurable at a session granularity. While some work on programmer interaction (Barke et al., 2023), AI-induced security regressions (Perry et al., 2023), and cognitive-offloading probes (Aiersilan (2026)) sketches this, it is an open question whether architecture can respond to such measurements via mechanisms like generator–evaluator separation (Rajasekaran, 2026) or comprehension-preserving surfaces.

In terms of taxonomy, AI coding tools are categorized by autonomy: Inline completion (e.g., GitHub Copilot), Chat-integrated (e.g., Cursor, Windsurf, Cody), Agentic CLI (e.g., Claude Code, Codex CLI, Aider), and Fully autonomous (e.g., Devin, SWE-Agent, OpenHands). Claude Code follows the ReAct pattern (reasoning, tool invocation, execution, result feedback) and uses up to 54 built-in tools and a layered permission system, fitting into a broader design space of planning, memory, and tool use (Weng, 2023) and trade-offs between autonomy, controllability, latency, accuracy, capability, and reliability (Xu, 2026).
