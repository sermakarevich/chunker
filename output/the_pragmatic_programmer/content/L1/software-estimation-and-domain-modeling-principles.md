# software-estimation-and-domain-modeling-principles

**Parent:** [[content/L2/architectural-principles-and-risk-management|architectural-principles-and-risk-management]] — The context details advanced principles for software architecture, including Orthogonality (decoupling components like DB from UI) and Reversibility (abstracting services like persistence). It contrasts two risk management techniques: Tracers (structural, permanent skeleton) and Prototypes (disposable, for learning/exploration). It also provides structured guidance on project estimation, requiring adherence to specific time unit scaling rules and the use of model-building and multi-variable analysis.

The definition and execution of a software system require consideration of linguistic boundaries, encompassing both the application's inherent domain language and the limitations of the programming language used. Programmers face the challenge that the boundaries of their chosen programming language (e.g., C++, Haskell-style thinking) and the actual language of the problem domain define the limits of their thinking and communication capabilities. Programming languages introduce inherent features, such as various buzzwords—including static versus dynamic typing, early versus late binding, functional versus object-oriented (OO) paradigms, inheritance models, mixins, and macros—all of which have the potential to suggest or obscure effective solutions. For instance, designing a solution based on C++ principles will inherently yield a different result compared to one derived from Haskell-style thinking, and this variance holds true vice versa. To ensure professional rigor, the guiding principle is that developers should prioritize utilizing the vocabulary of the actual application domain. At an advanced level, highly skilled developers, referred to as Pragmatic Programmers, may even program using the specific vocabulary, syntax, and semantics of the domain itself. One structured method for achieving this is the use of internal domain languages, exemplified by RSpec, a testing library for Ruby that subsequently inspired versions for many modern programming languages. An RSpec test utilizes the structural format: `describe <Context> do it`. 

Parallel to the discussion of domain modeling, the concept of 'Estimating' in software development involves the critical skill of calculating potential metrics related to time, storage, or performance, which allows a professional to gauge the feasibility and scope of a system's domain. When embarking on any estimate, the foundational step is to understand the specific context in which the answer will be utilized, determining whether a high degree of accuracy is required or if a rough ballpark figure will suffice. The choice of units for the estimate is particularly crucial; for example, stating a duration of '130 working days' conveys a significantly higher implied degree of accuracy than stating 'about six months,' even if both figures describe the identical total duration.

To appropriately scale time estimates, adherence to defined guidelines is essential: 1–15 days should be quoted in Days; 3–6 weeks should be quoted in Weeks; 8–20 weeks should be quoted in Months; and 20+ weeks should be quoted in Months. This rule dictates, for example, that if a project is calculated to take 125 working days (which equals 25 weeks), the professional must communicate this estimate as 'about six months.'

All estimations are fundamentally constructed upon models of the problem. Before developing complex models, a highly effective basic trick is to consult individuals who have successfully solved similar problems previously. While an exact match for the initial problem is improbable, the experiences of others can be successfully adapted and drawn upon. The initial phase of estimation requires building a thorough understanding of the domain and the precise scope of the question. Often, the initially stated scope becomes an integrated component of the final answer; for example, one might estimate the time to reach a specific destination by making implicit assumptions, such as 'there are no traffic accidents and there’s gas in the car.'

To systematically construct a model of the system, the professional must create a rudimentary, bare-bones mental model, guided by the initial understanding of the question. This process may involve conceptualizing elements like a server and the arrival of traffic when response times are being estimated, or mapping out the distinct steps an organization follows during the development lifecycle for a project. Developing this model is highly beneficial because it frequently enables the discovery of underlying patterns and processes that were initially not visible. By going through the model-building process, the professional can reexamine the original question, potentially realizing that a simpler variation, such as 'Y, a variant of X,' could be accomplished in roughly half the time while only sacrificing a single feature.

Once the core model is established, the next critical step is to decompose it into smaller, manageable components. The primary goal here is the discovery of the mathematical rules that govern the interaction among these components. Some components may contribute a single added value, while others might supply multiplicative factors, and some could be highly complex, such as those simulating the arrival rate of traffic at a specific node. For every single component, the professional must accurately identify its necessary parameters. Subsequently, the professional must assign a specific value to each parameter, dedicating special focus to those parameters that possess the greatest impact on the ultimate result. Parameters that are multiplied or divided are typically considered more significant than those that are merely added to a total. To calculate these critical parameters, the professional must develop a verifiable and justifiable method, such as measuring the actual transaction arrival rate from an existing operational system or locating a similar existing system for measurement. Since the estimation process often involves building one estimate upon others, this reliance point represents the largest potential source of errors. 

Following the completion of calculations, the professional must recognize that complex systems rarely yield a singular, definitive answer. Instead, it is necessary to execute multiple calculations, deliberately varying the values of the critical parameters, until the true driving parameters are conclusively identified. Utilizing a spreadsheet program can significantly aid this multi-variable process. The final answer should be presented in explicit terms of these identified parameters. For example, stating that 'The response time is roughly three quarters of a second if the system has SSDs and 32GB of memory, and one second with 16GB memory' provides a much richer sense of accuracy than simply stating '750ms.' If the arithmetic calculations are proven correct, yet the resulting answer appears nonsensical, the professional should immediately deduce that the foundational understanding of the problem or the model employed is likely flawed—this realization itself is a valuable piece of information.

To systematically enhance the accuracy of subsequent estimates, individuals are strongly advised to maintain a detailed log of their predictions, meticulously tracking how accurate the final results were. When an overall estimate requires the calculation of numerous subestimates, the professional must concurrently maintain a detailed record of these subestimates. The primary goal of this logging is the careful observation of the degree of accuracy in the predictions. If an estimate proves incorrect, the professional must not disregard the failure; instead, the failure must be treated as an investigative opportunity. Potential sources for an error include selecting parameters that do not correspond to the problem's reality or relying on an incorrect systemic model. Uncovering the precise, root cause of the error is crucial, as this investigation will necessarily lead to significantly better subsequent estimations. 

### ESTIMATING PROJECT SCHEDULES

Project scheduling estimates typically involve determining the anticipated duration required for complex tasks, a process recognized as inherently challenging to accurately predict. To manage this persistent uncertainty, two primary and established techniques are utilized.

**Painting the Missile (Scenario-Based Estimation)**
In typical real-world scenarios, people rarely estimate duration using a single numerical figure; rather, they furnish a range of possibilities that encompass different operational scenarios. For instance, estimating the time required to paint a house might generate a low estimate (e.g., 10 hours, assuming ideal conditions and perfect paint coverage), a more moderate, realistic estimate (e.g., 18 hours), and a highly pessimistic estimate (e.g., 30 hours or more, necessitated by adverse weather conditions).

The U.S. Navy adopted this methodology when planning the Polaris submarine project, formalizing it under the name Program Evaluation Review Technique (PERT). Each distinct task requiring estimation within a PERT framework mandates three separate estimates: an optimistic figure, a most likely figure, and a pessimistic figure. The professional is required to arrange these tasks into a dependency network and then use fundamental statistics to calculate the most likely and least likely possible times for the entirety of the project. Employing a comprehensive range of values helps to avoid a common and damaging source of estimation error: the act of padding the estimate merely out of generalized uncertainty. Instead, the statistical principles underpinning PERT effectively distribute the uncertainty across the entire scope of the project, which consequently results in substantially improved estimates for the whole scope.

**Eating the Elephant (Iterative Estimation)**
The most reliable methodology for determining a project's timeline involves acquiring direct, practical experience on the project itself. This necessity does not create a paradox if the professional adheres to the practice of incremental development. Incremental development requires repeatedly completing the following detailed cycle using very small, isolated slices of functionality: checking requirements, performing risk analysis (with a priority given to the riskiest items early), designing, implementing, integrating, and validating the functionality with the users. 

Initially, the professional might only have a general idea regarding the total number of iterations required or the approximate duration of each one. While some specialized methodologies might mandate nailing down this number during the initial planning phase, this practice is incorrect for all projects except those deemed the most trivial. Unless the project is a direct mimicry of a previous project that utilized the same team and technology stack, guessing the timeline is highly unreliable. Consequently, the professional must complete the coding and testing of the initial functionality and formally mark this as the conclusion of the first iteration. Based solely on this accrued practical experience, the professional can then refine the initial guess concerning both the number of required iterations and the scope of work for each subsequent one. This refinement process improves with every single cycle, and the professional's confidence in the final schedule grows in direct correlation with the refinement. This method should be formally executed during the team’s comprehensive review at the conclusion of each iterative cycle, thereby paralleling the principle of eating an elephant one bite at a time. The professional should formally refine the schedule as an integral part of every iteration, which helps management understand that the final schedule will be determined by the team's actual productivity, the working environment, and the overall effort expended, thereby providing the most accurate scheduling estimates possible. The optimal time to communicate the schedule to stakeholders is to respond with, “I’ll get back to you.”

For *any* estimate, the professional must allocate adequate time to slow down and carefully work through the descriptive steps provided in this document, because estimates given under duress (such as at a coffee machine) are frequently inaccurate.

**RECOMMENDED ACTIONS:**
*   **Logging:** The professional must start keeping a detailed log of all estimates, rigorously tracking the accuracy of every prediction. If the observed error exceeds 50%, the professional is obligated to investigate the specific causes of the inaccuracy.
*   **Communication:** When asked for an estimate, the professional's standard response should be, “I’ll get back to you.”
