# engineering-daybooks-and-defensive-coding

**Parent:** [[content/L1/professional-software-engineering-workflows|professional-software-engineering-workflows]] — Professional mastery requires mastering specialized tools and methodologies, including the systematic use of PERT (optimistic, likely, pessimistic estimates) and iterative development to manage project uncertainty; developers must achieve 'editor fluency' and leverage specialized text manipulation languages like Python and Ruby to build complex workflows, while defensive programming principles, notably Design by Contract (DBC) defined by Bertrand Meyer, mandate explicitly guaranteeing module boundaries and expected states.

The concept of an Engineering Daybook, which is a kind of journal, was utilized by early electronic and mechanical engineers at a small computer manufacturer. These engineers would carry a physical notebook, often with a pen stuffed down the spine, where they would write down their work notes. This daybook serves as a record of activities, learned concepts, ideas sketched in the margins, readings from meters, and generally, anything related to the work being performed. When the notebook became full, the engineers would write the date range on the spine and file it next to previous daybooks. Developers can use daybooks to take meeting notes, jot down current project status, record variable values during debugging, leave reminders, record wild ideas, and occasionally for doodling. The daybook offers three primary benefits: first, it is more reliable than human memory, allowing a developer to recall specific details, such as the name and phone number of a company called the previous week regarding a power supply problem; second, the daybook provides a place to store ideas that are not immediately relevant to the current task, enabling the developer to concentrate on the main task while knowing that the great idea is preserved; and third, the daybook acts as a kind of rubber duck, allowing the brain to switch gears when the developer stops to write something down. This reflection process can sometimes lead to realizing that the topic being noted is incorrect. An additional benefit is the ability to look back at past work, recalling people, projects, and aesthetics from many years ago. The text suggests maintaining an engineering daybook using paper, rather than a digital file or wiki, because the act of writing differs significantly from typing. Developing the habit for one month may yield benefits. If no specific benefit is observed, the practice may simplify writing one's memoir later in life. The philosophy of pragmatic programming emphasizes writing defensively because perfect software does not exist. Because no developer has ever written perfectly functional software in the brief history of computing, accepting this reality as a fact is necessary to prevent wasting time and energy chasing an impossible dream. Rather than focusing on achieving perfection, a pragmatic programmer should approach software development defensively. Since developers are constantly interfacing with other people’s code—code that might not meet high standards—and dealing with inputs that may or may not be valid, developers must adopt defensive coding practices. These practices include validating all provided information, using assertions to detect bad data, distrusting data from potential attackers or trolls, checking for consistency, and applying constraints on database columns. Furthermore, the text recommends that programmers build in defenses against their own mistakes, acknowledging that no person writes perfect code, including themselves. Defensive measures include using Design by Contract (Topic 23) to ensure that clients and suppliers agree on rights and responsibilities, applying Dead Programs Tell No Lies (Topic 24) to ensure that work remains undamaged during bug fixing, using Assertive Programming (Topic 25) to actively verify assumptions as the code runs, employing resources balancing techniques (Topic 26) when dealing with system resources like memory or files, and always proceeding in small steps as outlined in Topic 27, Don’t Outrun Your Headlights. Embracing this defensive approach ensures that developers remain safe in a world of imperfect systems, ridiculous timelines, laughable tools, and impossible requirements.
