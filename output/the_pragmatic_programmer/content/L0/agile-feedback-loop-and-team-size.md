# agile-feedback-loop-and-team-size

**Parent:** [[content/L1/adaptive-development-and-collaboration|adaptive-development-and-collaboration]] — Effective development mandates continuous adaptation through a three-step feedback loop (status identification, smallest meaningful step, evaluation/fixing), and requires collaborative methods like Pair or Mob Programming, which should be applied across all roles and ideally include users. Technical mastery requires deep fluency in tools (e.g., command shells, editors), mastering estimation via PERT/Iterative methods, and adopting advanced architectural patterns like PubSub, Reactive Programming, and the Actor Model.

The process of software development is not dictated by predefined rules; rather, decisions must be contextual, depending on factors like the identity of the developers, the nature of the team, the application, the tools, the company, the customer, and the surrounding environment. Because of this vast uncertainty, no fixed, static plan can guarantee success. Therefore, the recommended approach is to continuously manage uncertainty by actively gathering and acting on feedback. This feedback loop involves three iterative steps: 1) identifying the current status, 2) making the smallest meaningful step toward the desired goal, and 3) evaluating the result to fix any broken components. Teams should repeat these steps recursively at every level of work. The concept is so powerful that even a minor detail, such as renaming a variable from `user` to `owner` and subsequently optimizing it to `email` in the example `let email = emailOfAccountOwner(accountID);`, can improve the overall system design by reducing the coupling between the code and the account management functionality. Furthermore, this feedback loop applies even at the highest level of a project; successful work has occurred when starting with a client’s requirements, taking a single step, and realizing that the best solution may not require any software at all. This loop must also apply to a team's process review. Furthermore, the ability to efficiently fix broken components (Step 3) requires practicing good design, as good design makes things easier to change. This necessity for easy changeability is what constitutes agility. Beyond software, the simple feedback loop can improve any decision; the goal is to consider ways to undo recent decisions if the desired direction changes. For projects involving multiple people, the optimal team structure is small and relatively stable, ideally having fewer than 10-12 members, as large groups are more accurately described as a horde.
