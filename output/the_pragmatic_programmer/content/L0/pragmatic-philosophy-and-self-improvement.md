# pragmatic-philosophy-and-self-improvement

**Parent:** [[content/L1/pragmatic-programmer-philosophy-and-career-advancement|pragmatic-programmer-philosophy-and-career-advancement]] — The Pragmatic Programmer must cultivate key traits like being an early adopter, an inquisitive and critical thinker, and having a realistic, 'jack of all trades' approach. Furthermore, the philosophy emphasizes taking full responsibility for one's career, providing solutions instead of excuses, and preventing 'software rot' by maintaining meticulous care for their work and professional environment.

The book, *The Pragmatic Programmer: Your Journey to Mastery*, is intended for people who wish to become more effective and productive programmers. Readers may feel frustrated about not achieving their potential, or they might observe colleagues using tools that increase their productivity. Additionally, the book addresses situations where a reader's current job uses older technologies and wants to learn how modern ideas can be applied to the reader's work.

While the authors acknowledge that they do not possess all (or even most) of the answers, and not all of the ideas are applicable in all situations, the authors state that following the book's approach will allow readers to rapidly gain experience, increase productivity, improve understanding of the entire development process, and write better software.

***What Makes a Pragmatic Programmer?***

Individual developers possess unique strengths, weaknesses, preferences, and dislikes, and over time, each developer will craft a personal environment that reflects their individuality. However, individuals defined as Pragmatic Programmers tend to share several key characteristics:

*   **Early adopter/fast adapter:** A Pragmatic Programmer has an instinct for technologies and techniques, enjoys trying out new things, and when presented with something new, the programmer can grasp it quickly and integrate it with the rest of the programmer's knowledge. This confidence is built from experience.
*   **Inquisitive:** A Pragmatic Programmer tends to ask questions, such as “How did you do that? Did you have problems with that library? What’s this quantum computing I’ve heard about? How are symbolic links implemented?” The inquisitive programmer is a collector of little facts, each of which may influence a decision years in the future.
*   **Critical thinker:** A Pragmatic Programmer rarely accepts things as given without first obtaining the facts. When colleagues state, “because that’s the way it’s done,” or a vendor promises a solution to all problems, the Pragmatic Programmer suspects a challenge.
*   **Realistic:** The Pragmatic Programmer strives to understand the underlying nature of every problem encountered. This realism gives the programmer a good sense of how difficult the task is and how long the task will take. Deeply understanding that a process will be difficult or require significant time gives the programmer the stamina necessary to continue.
*   **Jack of all trades:** A Pragmatic Programmer works hard to be familiar with a broad range of technologies and environments and continues working to stay current with new developments. Even if the programmer's current job requires specialization, the programmer will always be able to move toward new areas and new challenges.

Beyond these core characteristics, all Pragmatic Programmers share foundational tips:

*   **Tip 1: Care About Your Craft:** The authors believe that developing software is pointless unless the programmer cares about doing it well.
*   **Tip 2: Think! About Your Work:** To be a Pragmatic Programmer, the reader must engage in ongoing critical appraisal of every decision made, every day, on every project, instead of running on auto-pilot. Thinking and critically appraising the work constantly is the Pragmatic Programmer’s mantra, echoing the old IBM corporate motto. If this requires effort, the programmer is exhibiting the realistic characteristic. While this requires valuable time—which is often under pressure—the benefits include more active involvement with a job the programmer loves, a feeling of mastery over an increasing range of subjects, and pleasure derived from continuous improvement. Over the long term, this time investment will be repaid as both the programmer and the team become more efficient, write code that is easier to maintain, and reduce time spent in meetings.

***Individual Pragmatists, Large Teams***

Some individuals believe that large teams or complex projects eliminate room for individuality, asserting that “Software is an engineering discipline,” and “breaks down if individual team members make decisions for themselves.” The authors strongly disagree with this view. While engineering is necessary in software construction, this does not eliminate individual craftsmanship. The authors cite the example of large cathedrals built in Europe during the Middle Ages, which took thousands of person-years of effort over many decades. While lessons in structural engineering were passed down, the carpenters, stonecutters, carvers, and glass workers were all craftspeople whose belief in their individual contributions sustained the projects. Therefore, within the overall structure of a project, there is always room for individuality and craftsmanship, a truth particularly relevant to the current state of software engineering. The authors suggest that one hundred years from now, software engineering may seem as archaic as medieval techniques to current civil engineers, while craftsmanship will still be honored.

***It’s a Continuous Process***

Great programmers, much like great lawns, require small amounts of daily care. The authors introduce *kaizen*, a Japanese term signifying the concept of continuously making many small improvements. This concept was historically linked to dramatic gains in productivity and quality in Japanese manufacturing and was widely adopted globally. *Kaizen* applies to individuals as well; every day, the reader should work to refine skills and add new tools to the repertoire. Unlike the Eton lawns, which require centuries of effort, the reader will start seeing results in a matter of days, and over the years, the reader will observe how experience and skills have grown.

***A Pragmatic Philosophy***

The book emphasizes that a reader’s career is foremost and that the core philosophy is Topic 1, It’s Your Life. The authors state that Pragmatic Programmers are distinguished by their attitude, style, and philosophy for approaching problems and solutions. Pragmatic Programmers think beyond the immediate problem, placing it within its larger context to seek the bigger picture, which is necessary for making intelligent compromises and informed decisions. Furthermore, Pragmatic Programmers take responsibility for everything they do (discussed in Topic 2, The Cat Ate My Source Code) and will not passively allow their projects to fail through neglect (Topic 3, Software Entropy). The authors describe understanding the context of one's work as beneficial because it helps determine how good the software needs to be; while sometimes near-perfection is required, trade-offs are often involved (Topic 5, Good-Enough Software). To execute this philosophy, the reader needs a broad base of knowledge and experience (Topic 6, Your Knowledge Portfolio). Finally, because no programmer works in isolation, the book dedicates Topic 7, Communicate! to methods for interacting with others better. Pragmatic programming, therefore, originates from a philosophy of pragmatic thinking, forming the basis of this chapter.

***Topic 1: It’s Your Life***

The authors observe that many developers feel frustrated—some feel stagnant in their jobs, and others feel that technology has passed them by. Other concerns include feeling underappreciated, underpaid, or working with toxic teams. Many developers seek change, such as moving to Asia, Europe, or working remotely. However, the authors offer the same response: “Why can’t you change it?” They argue that software development must be perceived as being near the top of any career list regarding the control a professional has. Since skills are in demand, knowledge crosses geographical boundaries, and remote work is possible, developers can achieve nearly anything they want.

Despite this, the authors note that developers often resist change, remaining passive as their skills become dated, and complaining that companies do not provide adequate training. The most important tip provided is Tip 3: You Have Agency. The authors advise readers that if the work environment is unsatisfactory or the job is boring, the reader should try to fix it, but not indefinitely. As Martin Fowler stated, “you can change your organization or change your organization.” If technology seems to be passing the reader by, the reader should make time (using personal time) to study new, interesting subjects, viewing this as an investment in oneself. Furthermore, regarding remote work, the reader should ask for it; if the workplace says no, the reader should seek out an organization that says yes.
