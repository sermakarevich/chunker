# taking-responsibility-and-options

**Parent:** [[content/L1/pragmatic-programmer-philosophy-and-career-advancement|pragmatic-programmer-philosophy-and-career-advancement]] — The Pragmatic Programmer must cultivate key traits like being an early adopter, an inquisitive and critical thinker, and having a realistic, 'jack of all trades' approach. Furthermore, the philosophy emphasizes taking full responsibility for one's career, providing solutions instead of excuses, and preventing 'software rot' by maintaining meticulous care for their work and professional environment.

One of the cornerstones of the pragmatic philosophy is the idea that programmers must take responsibility for their own career advancement, their learning and education, their projects, and their day-to-day work. Programmers take charge of their own careers and are not afraid to admit ignorance or error. While admitting mistakes is not the most pleasant aspect of programming—and despite thorough testing, good documentation, and solid automation—things will inevitably go wrong, such as late deliverables or unforeseen technical problems. The professional way to handle these situations means practicing honesty and directness; while programmers can be proud of their abilities, they must also own up to their shortcomings, including ignorance and mistakes. TEAM TRUST is paramount because, in a healthy environment based in trust, team members can safely speak their minds, present their ideas, and rely on their team members who can in turn rely on them. Without this mutual trust, the working environment suffers. For instance, if a high-tech, stealth ninja team infiltrates a villain’s evil lair, and one team member reports, “Sorry, folks, I don’t have the laser. The cat was playing with the red dot and I left it at home,” that sort of breach of trust might be very difficult to repair. REGARDING TAKE RESPONSIBILITY: Responsibility is an active agreement and involves making a commitment to ensuring that something is done right. While an individual does not necessarily have direct control over every aspect of a commitment, the individual must also analyze the situation for risks that are beyond the individual's control. An individual has the right not to accept responsibility for an impossible situation, a situation where the risks are too great, or one with ethically sketchy implications; the individual must make this call based on personal values and judgment. When an individual accepts responsibility for an outcome, the individual should expect to be held accountable for that outcome. When making a mistake or an error in judgment, the individual must admit it honestly and attempt to offer solutions. The individual must not blame someone or something else, nor make up an excuse; the individual should not blame all problems on a vendor, a programming language, management, or coworkers, even though any and all of these may play a role. Instead, it is the individual's responsibility to provide solutions, not excuses. For example, if a vendor fails to deliver, the individual should have developed a contingency plan. If a mass storage unit melts, taking all source code with it, and the individual lacks a backup, the fault rests with the individual. 

**Tip 4: Provide Options, Don’t Make Lame Excuses**

Before approaching anyone to explain why something cannot be done, is late, or is broken, the individual must stop and listen to self-talk. The individual should ask if the excuse sounds reasonable or stupid. The individual should run through the conversation mentally, considering what the other person is likely to say (e.g., “Have you tried this…” or “Didn’t you consider that?”) and how the individual will respond. Before delivering bad news, the individual should consider if there are any alternative solutions. Instead of excuses, the individual must provide options; instead of saying something cannot be done, the individual must explain what can be done to salvage the situation. The individual should consider whether the code has to be deleted or if explaining the value of refactoring (see Topic 40, Refactoring) is appropriate. Alternatives could include spending time prototyping to determine the best way to proceed (see Topic 13, Prototypes and Post-it Notes), introducing better testing (see Topic 41, Test to Code, and Ruthless and Continuous Testing), or implementing automation to prevent future issues. Other options may involve acquiring additional resources to complete the task, spending more time with the users, or recognizing the need for the individual to learn a technique or technology in greater depth; in these cases, the individual should not be afraid to ask for help. The individual should flush out potential lame excuses before voicing them aloud. If excuses must be used, the individual should tell a cat first. 

**CHALLENGES**

When encountering someone—such as a bank teller, an auto mechanic, or a clerk—with a lame excuse, the individual must consider how the person and the company will be viewed. When the individual finds that it is necessary to say, “I don’t know,” the individual must follow up with “—but I’ll find out” to acknowledge the gap in knowledge while simultaneously taking professional responsibility.
