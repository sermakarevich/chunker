# software-entropy-and-broken-windows

**Parent:** [[content/L1/pragmatic-programmer-philosophy-and-career-advancement|pragmatic-programmer-philosophy-and-career-advancement]] — The Pragmatic Programmer must cultivate key traits like being an early adopter, an inquisitive and critical thinker, and having a realistic, 'jack of all trades' approach. Furthermore, the philosophy emphasizes taking full responsibility for one's career, providing solutions instead of excuses, and preventing 'software rot' by maintaining meticulous care for their work and professional environment.

Topic 3: Software Entropy. While software development is immune from almost all physical laws, the inexorable increase in entropy, which is a term from physics referring to the amount of "disorder" in a system, hits software development hard. When disorder increases in software, the phenomenon is called "software rot." Some individuals might call this problem the more optimistic term, "technical debt," suggesting the implied notion that the team will pay it back someday; however, the text suggests that the team probably will not pay the debt. Regardless of whether the name is debt or rot, both can spread uncontrollably.

Contributing to software rot are many factors, but the most important one appears to be the psychology, or culture, at work on a project. Even a team of one must maintain a delicate project psychology. Despite excellent plans and talented people, a project can still experience ruin and decay during its lifetime. Conversely, other projects, even those facing enormous difficulties and constant setbacks, successfully fight nature’s tendency toward disorder and manage to maintain a good state of functionality. The difference can be understood by referencing inner cities where some buildings are beautiful and clean, while others are rotting hulks. Researchers in the field of crime and urban decay discovered a fascinating trigger mechanism: a single broken window. When one window is left unrepaired for any substantial length of time, the broken window instills in the inhabitants a sense of abandonment—the feeling that the governing powers do not care about the building. This sense of abandonment then leads to other windows being broken, littering, graffiti, and eventually serious structural damage. In a relatively short span of time, the building becomes damaged beyond the owner’s desire to fix it, and the sense of abandonment becomes a reality.

Psychologists have conducted studies that show hopelessness can be contagious. Ignoring a clearly broken situation reinforces the idea that nothing can be fixed, that no one cares, and that all is doomed. These negative thoughts can spread among team members, creating a vicious spiral.

**Tip 5: Don’t Live with Broken Windows.** The individual should not allow "broken windows" (which include bad designs, wrong decisions, or poor code) to remain unrepaired. Instead, one must fix each issue as soon as it is discovered. If insufficient time exists to fix the issue properly, the individual should board it up; possible actions include commenting out the offending code, displaying a “Not Implemented” message, or substituting dummy data instead. Taking some action prevents further damage and demonstrates that the individual remains in control of the situation. The text notes that clean, functional systems deteriorate quickly once windows begin breaking. While other factors can contribute to software rot, neglect accelerates the process faster than any other factor. If an individual believes no one has time to clean up all the broken glass of a project, the individual should plan on acquiring a dumpster or moving to a different neighborhood, emphasizing the need to prevent entropy from winning.

Andy once encountered an acquaintance whose house was immaculate and loaded with priceless antiques, objets d’art, and similar items. One day, a tapestry hanging too close to a fireplace caught fire. The fire department rushed in to save the house, but before dragging dirty hoses into the house, the fire department stopped—even with the fire raging—to roll out a mat between the front door and the source of the fire. The fire department did not want to damage the carpet. This demonstrates that the fire department assessed the situation, was confident of its ability to manage the fire, and was careful not to inflict unnecessary damage to the property. Software development must operate this way: the individual must not cause collateral damage simply because a crisis exists. One broken window, such as badly designed code or a poor management decision that the team must live with for the duration of the project, is enough to start the decline. If an individual finds themselves working on a project with quite a few broken windows, it is very easy to adopt the mindset that "All the rest of this code is crap, I’ll just follow suit." This is true even if the project had been fine up to that point. For example, in the original experiment leading to the “Broken Window Theory,” an abandoned car sat for a week untouched, but once a single window was broken, the car was stripped and turned upside down within hours. Similarly, if the code is pristine—cleanly written, well designed, and elegant—the individual will likely take extra special care not to mess it up, much like the firefighters. Even if a crisis is raging (like a deadline, release date, or trade show demonstration), the individual should avoid making a mess and inflicting additional damage. The individual must tell themselves, "No broken windows." The text notes that related sections include Topic 10, Orthogonality.
