# collaborative-programming-techniques

**Parent:** [[content/L1/adaptive-development-and-collaboration|adaptive-development-and-collaboration]] — Effective development mandates continuous adaptation through a three-step feedback loop (status identification, smallest meaningful step, evaluation/fixing), and requires collaborative methods like Pair or Mob Programming, which should be applied across all roles and ideally include users. Technical mastery requires deep fluency in tools (e.g., command shells, editors), mastering estimation via PERT/Iterative methods, and adopting advanced architectural patterns like PubSub, Reactive Programming, and the Actor Model.

Effective collaboration in software development, which supersedes formal methods like endless meetings, memos, or overly detailed legal documentation, involves combining discussion and question-asking with active coding. This practice is significantly more powerful than merely discussing a problem or taking notes. Conway's Law, introduced by Melvin Conway in 1967, dictates that organizations designing systems are constrained to produce designs that mirror the communication structures of those organizations. Specifically, the social structures and communication pathways within a team or organization will be reflected in the resulting application, website, or product. Studies support this, showing, for instance, that teams lacking communication will result in siloed, 'stove-pipe' systems, while teams split into two may produce a client/server or frontend/backend division. Furthermore, deliberate structuring of the team can influence the code: geographically distributed teams tend toward more modular, distributed software, and development teams that include users will produce software that reflects that involvement, while teams that do not bother reflecting user involvement will also produce flawed software. 

**Pair Programming**
Pair programming is a widely adopted practice of eXtreme Programming. In this process, one developer operates the keyboard (the typist), while the second developer does not. Both developers work on the problem together and can switch typing duties as needed. Pairing offers several benefits because different people contribute varied backgrounds, experiences, problem-solving techniques, approaches, and levels of focus. The developer acting as typist must focus on low-level syntax and coding style details, allowing the other developer to consider higher-level issues and scope. Since human brain bandwidth is limited, having a second developer's full attention available during the task significantly increases mental power. Additionally, the inherent peer-pressure from the second person helps prevent momentary lapses or poor naming habits, which elevates software quality. 

**Mob Programming**
Mob programming is an extension of pair programming that involves more than two developers collaborating on the same problem simultaneously, using one typist. Proponents report that mobs can achieve great results when solving hard problems and can easily include people typically outside the core development team, such as users, project sponsors, and testers. For instance, during one project, a small mob of three was common, consisting of one developer typing while the other discussed the issue with a business expert. 

**Implementing Collaboration**
Developers should try pair programming if currently working solo, giving it a minimum of two weeks for only a few hours at a time, as it may initially feel strange. For brainstorming new ideas or diagnosing complex issues, trying a mob programming session may be beneficial. If a team is already pairing or mobbing, members should consider extending participation beyond just developers to include users, testers, or sponsors. 

As with all collaboration, managing the human aspects is as crucial as managing the technical aspects. Recommendations include: building the code, not the ego; starting small (e.g., a mob with only 4-5 people, or starting with just a few pairs in short sessions); criticizing the code, not the person (e.g., stating, 'Let’s look at this block,' instead of 'you’re wrong'); and actively listening to and trying to understand others’ viewpoints, recognizing that 'different isn’t wrong.' The team should also conduct frequent retrospectives to improve for future efforts. 

In conclusion, coding in any setting—whether in the same office or remotely, alone, in pairs, or in mobs—constitutes an effective way to solve problems. If a team is accustomed to one style, the team might experiment with a different one. However, developers should not adopt a naive approach; they should research the rules, suggestions, and guidelines for each style. A final piece of advice is to remember that agility is an adjective describing *how* one performs tasks (e.g., 'an agile developer' or 'a team that responds to change with agility'), not a noun describing a person or process. The core values highlighted by the Agile Manifesto are: valuing individuals and interactions over processes and tools, working software over comprehensive documentation, customer collaboration over contract negotiation, and responding to change over following a plan.
