# functional-programming-in-scala-book-guide

**Parent:** [[content/L1/functional-programming-in-scala-context|functional-programming-in-scala-context]] — The book *Functional Programming in Scala* by Paul Chiusano and Rúnar Bjarnason, copyrighted in 2015 and published by Manning Shelter Island, is structured into four parts (starting on pages 1, 93, 173, and 227, respectively) and teaches FP principles by treating Scala as a purely functional language, detailing core concepts, functional design patterns, advanced abstractions, and finally, real-world I/O operations.

The book, *Functional Programming in Scala* by Paul Chiusano and Rúnar Bjarnason, published by Manning Shelter Island, details functional programming (FP) and is available online at www.manning.com. The materials are licensed to Emre Sevinc via <emre.sevinc@gmail.com>.

**Intended Audience and Approach**
This book is designed for readers who possess at least some prior programming experience. While the authors wrote the book with an intermediate-level Java or C programmer in mind, they believe the book is suitable for programmers coming from any language and at any experience level. The authors emphasize that motivation and curiosity are more important than prior expertise. Because functional programming is a challenging topic, it requires a radically different way of thinking, and readers should prepare to be beginners, regardless of their professional experience.

Although the book does not require prior experience with Scala, the authors will not spend significant time discussing Scala’s syntax or language features. Instead, they will introduce these concepts minimally, mostly as a consequence of covering other material. If readers have further questions about the Scala language, they should supplement their reading by using another book on Scala (http://scala-lang.org/documentation/books.html) or by looking up specific questions in the Scala language documentation (http://scala-lang.org/documentation/).

**Structure and Reading Guide**
Readers can read the book sequentially, but the organization of the four parts is designed to allow comfortable breaks, enabling the reader to apply learned concepts to their own projects and revisit the material later. Specifically, the material in Part 4 will make the most sense after the reader has established a strong familiarity with the functional programming style introduced in Parts 1, 2, and 3. After completing Part 3, the reader may consider taking a break to practice writing functional programs beyond the in-chapter exercises.

Most chapters follow a similar structure: the book introduces a new idea or technique, explains it with an example, and then works through a number of exercises. The authors strongly suggest that readers download the exercise source code and perform the exercises as they progress through each chapter. Exercises, hints, and answers are available at https://github.com/fpinscala/fpinscla. Readers are also encouraged to visit the scala-functional Google group (https://groups.google.com/forum/#!topic/scala-functional/) and the #fp-in-scala IRC channel on irc.freenode.net for discussions and questions.
