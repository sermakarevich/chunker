The persistence of agent state and the human–agent working relationship across sessions involves two distinct architectural levels. One level, detailed in Section 7, documents the four-level CLAUDE.md hierarchy and automatic memory functions. The second level, detailed in Section 9, manages session-scoped transcripts in mostly append-only JSONL files, noting that the system does not restore session-scoped permissions upon reopening. The core challenge lies in defining a third layer of durable state: information that is neither a static instruction set nor a single session's transcript. Researchers have proposed several mechanisms for this accumulating layer, including those by Hu et al. (2025) and Zhang et al. (2025a), who motivate such an accumulation. Other proposed solutions include treating the Large Language Model (LLM) as an operating system with paged memory (Packer et al., 2023), building a production-oriented memory store that survives restarts (Chhikara et al., 2025), or designing a research agentic-memory system (Xu et al., 2025). Furthermore, specific techniques have been proposed: Wang et al. (2024c) captured reusable procedural traces, Shinn et al. (2023) accumulated self-reflection traces through verbal reinforcement across attempts, and surveys by Zhang et al. (2025b) and Huang et al. (2026) have mapped various candidate mechanisms. 

This persistence question extends to the human collaborator. Existing research on longitudinal autonomy includes studies by Huang et al. (2025) and McCain et al. (2026). Furthermore, field experiments by Dell’Acqua et al. (2025), conducted with 776 Procter & Gamble professionals, and longitudinal studies of Copilot rollouts (Stray et al., 2025) and AI-teamwork trajectories (Xiao et al., 2025), report observable shifts in human–AI work dynamics as collaboration accumulates. Additional works include Wang et al. (2023), who illustrated an embodied agent accumulating a skill library across tasks, and Mollick (2024), who framed the human–AI working relationship as co-intelligence.

Two open architectural questions persist regarding these design choices: First, whether a single substrate can successfully carry both a user’s personal instruction hierarchy and a shared organizational context while maintaining the file-based transparency of the CLAUDE.md files, as documented in Section 7. Second, how session-scoped permissions can interact with such a durable substrate without reintroducing the resume-restoration concern, which the system explicitly closes as a deliberate safety measure in Section 9.

Regarding Harness Boundary Evolution (Section 11.6), Rajasekaran (2026) observed that "the space of interesting harness combinations doesn’t shrink as models improve; it moves." This movement is not resolved by the source-level analysis provided in Sections 3 through 9. Researchers have addressed four dimensions: **Where** the harness operates; **When** it acts; **What** it acts on; and **With whom** it coordinates. 

*   **Where:** Martin et al. (2026) designed Managed Agents to virtualize the session, harness, and sandbox into independently replaceable interfaces, extending the virtual-memory analogy applied to context-window management (Packer et al., 2023) and popularized by Karpathy (2023); Khattab et al. (2023) treated the harness itself as a compile target. 
*   **When:** Chen et al. (2025) reported a task-pass gain of 12%–18% linked to proactivity, while Chen et al. (2025) also noted a sharp preference penalty (47% versus 80–90%) restricted to the high-frequency Persistent Suggest variant. Liu et al. (2025), Pu et al. (2025), and Lee et al. (2025) have extended the proactivity design space across programming and ambient-interface settings. Pasternak et al. (2025) and Sun et al. (2025) introduced benchmarks and training regimes to sharpen the proactivity field, and Deng et al. (2025) surveyed the broader landscape.
*   **What:** The work of Brohan et al. (2024) and Black et al. (2024) advanced Vision-language-action (VLA) policies that execute physical actions, and Ahn et al. (2022) grounded plans in robot affordances. Industry systems such as Figure AI (2025) and Bjorck et al. (2025) applied similar ideas to humanoid control. These systems must adhere to the reversibility-weighted risk principle (Table 1) at a cost asymmetry that the principle names but does not quantify for non-textual actions. 
*   **With whom:** Role-differentiated multi-agent systems, such as those developed by Hong et al. (2023), Li et al. (2023), Chen et al. (2023), and Qian et al. (2024), compose agents with distinct responsibilities. Further research includes multi-agent debate (Du et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2024) and graph-structured workflows (Zhuge et al., 2024) exploring alternatives to the parent/subagent pattern seen in Section 8, with Guo et al. (2024) surveying this space.
