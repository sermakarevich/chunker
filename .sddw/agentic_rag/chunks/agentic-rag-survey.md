Large Language Models (LLMs), such as GPT-5, PaLM, and LLaMA, have fundamentally transformed artificial intelligence by enabling human-like text generation and natural language understanding. However, a central challenge remains for LLMs: reliably reasoning over rapidly changing external knowledge rather than solely relying on static pre-training data. Despite these advances, LLMs remain constrained by static pre-training data, which often causes the models to produce outdated responses, hallucinated content [7], and to fail when adapting to evolving real-world information. 

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) addresses this limitation by integrating external knowledge into the generative process at inference time. By combining the LLM's generative capabilities with retrieval mechanisms [10] operating over vector databases [11], APIs, or web sources, RAG improves the factual grounding, relevance, and timeliness of the outputs. Traditional RAG pipelines, however, are typically static and linear, limiting complex multi-step reasoning, deep contextual understanding, and iterative response refinement. These limitations necessitate autonomous control mechanisms to dynamically decide when to retrieve information, how to reformulate queries, and when sufficient evidence has been collected.

In parallel, advancements in agentic AI have introduced intelligent agents capable of perception, reasoning, and autonomous task execution [13]. These agents utilize design patterns—reflection, planning, tool use, and multi-agent collaboration—to dynamically decompose tasks and adapt strategies. These capabilities are highly relevant to RAG because the decisions regarding retrieval and reasoning can themselves be treated as agentic tasks. These core patterns are further structured through workflow patterns [13, 14], including prompt chaining, routing, parallelization, orchestrator–worker models, and evaluator–optimizer loops, which govern how multiple prompts, models, or agents are coordinated at the system level.

The convergence of RAG and agentic intelligence has resulted in Agentic Retrieval-Augmented Generation (Agentic RAG) [15]. Agentic RAG integrates autonomous agents directly into the RAG pipeline to enable dynamic retrieval, iterative context refinement, and adaptive workflow orchestration [16]. This integration introduces an explicit control layer that guides how the system reasons over external evidence, coordinating retrieval, validating information, invoking tools, and refining responses. This capability makes Agentic RAG effective for complex, multi-domain tasks requiring precision and adaptability. 

Despite the rapid growth in interest, the Agentic RAG field remains fragmented, characterized by diverse architectures, inconsistent terminology, and a limited comparative understanding. Current research focuses on individual frameworks or application-specific implementations, and no unified taxonomy currently organizes Agentic RAG systems based on principled design dimensions. 

This paper, an analytical survey, aims to structure this emerging field. The authors review the evolution of RAG paradigms, including naïve, modular, and graph-based RAG [17], and their eventual transition toward agentic systems. Furthermore, the paper introduces a principled taxonomy of Agentic RAG architectures based on four criteria: agent cardinality, control structure, autonomy, and knowledge representation, followed by a comparative analysis of design trade-offs. The survey also examines applications in healthcare [18, 19], finance, and education [20], distills practical lessons for system designers, and identifies open research challenges to guide future work.

Section 7 examines applications of Agentic RAG, while Section 8 discusses implementation tools and frameworks. Section 9 provides a comparative analysis of Agentic RAG frameworks. Separately, the foundational stages of RAG evolution are described: 
*   **Naïve RAG [21]:** This foundational system relies on simple keyword-based retrieval techniques, such as TF-IDF and BM25, to fetch documents from static datasets. Naïve RAG is simple and easy to implement, making it suitable for fact-based queries lacking contextual complexity. However, it has limitations, including failure to capture semantic nuances (due to relying on lexical matching), resulting in fragmented or overly generic outputs, and struggling with large datasets during keyword-based retrieval.
