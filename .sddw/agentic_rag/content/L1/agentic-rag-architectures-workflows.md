# agentic-rag-architectures-workflows

**Parent:** [[index]]

The landscape of sophisticated AI systems, particularly in knowledge work automation and advanced information retrieval, is being dramatically reshaped by various agentic and adaptive architectures that extend beyond traditional Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). These advanced methodologies aim to provide not only context retrieval but also structured reasoning, state management, and end-to-end workflow automation, allowing systems to handle complex, multi-step processes across diverse domains.

### **Core Architectures for Advanced Retrieval**

**1. Agent-G: Agentic Framework for Graph RAG**
The Agent-G framework enhances RAG by fundamentally integrating graph knowledge bases with traditional unstructured document retrieval. Its core principle involves equipping a system with the capacity to dynamically assign retrieval tasks to specialized agents, allowing it to leverage both structured and unstructured data simultaneously. In this architecture, structured data (such as disease-to-symptom mappings in healthcare) is used to extract explicit relationships and hierarchies, while unstructured documents provide necessary contextual depth. The system employs a critical **Critic Module** which is vital for evaluating the relevance and overall quality of all retrieved information, thereby ensuring strict alignment with the original query. This process is reinforced by **Feedback Loops** that enable iterative validation and necessary re-querying, significantly improving the final output quality. The Agent-G workflow is managed by four primary components: a **Retriever Bank**, a modular collection of specialized agents; the **Critic Module** for quality control; **Dynamic Agent Interaction**, where task-specific agents collaborate to integrate diverse data types; and the LLM Integration stage, which synthesizes the validated data into a coherent response, maintaining continuous alignment with the query's intent. Key advantages include **Enhanced Reasoning** (combining relational graph data with contextual text), **Dynamic Adaptability**, **Improved Accuracy** (via the Critic Module), and **Scalable Modularity**.

**2. GeAR: Graph-Enhanced Agent for Retrieval-Augmented Generation**
GeAR is another specialized agentic framework that elevates RAG by systematically incorporating graph-based retrieval. It tackles the challenges inherent in multi-hop retrieval scenarios by utilizing two main innovations: **Graph Expansion** and an agent-based architecture. Graph Expansion is a technique that enhances conventional base retrievers (like BM25) by systematically expanding the retrieval scope to include relationships and dependencies between entities. This enables the system to capture complex, multi-step relationships. The GeAR workflow is driven by three components: a **Graph Expansion Module**, which integrates graph-based data to consider relationships during retrieval and expands the search space for multi-hop queries; an **Agent-Based Retrieval** component, which allows agents to dynamically select and combine retrieval strategies based on query complexity, autonomously deciding when to utilize graph-expanded paths; and the LLM Integration stage, which combines the graph-enriched information with generative capabilities for a contextually rich response. GeAR thus ensures that the generative output is informed by both the depth of unstructured documents and the precision of structured graph data.

**3. Adaptive RAG System Architecture**
Adaptive RAG focuses on maximizing the efficiency and accuracy of LLMs by dynamically adjusting the retrieval strategy based on the complexity of the incoming query. The system relies on a **Classifier Role**, a smaller language model that analyzes the query and predicts its complexity. This classifier is trained on automatically labeled datasets derived from past model outcomes and observed query patterns. Based on the assessment, the system executes one of three strategies: **Straightforward Queries** (fact-based questions requiring no retrieval; the LLM responds directly); **Simple Queries** (moderately complex tasks requiring a single-step retrieval); or **Complex Queries** (highly complex, multi-layered tasks activating multi-step retrieval for iterative refinement). The key advantages are **Dynamic Adaptability** (optimizing efficiency and accuracy), **Resource Efficiency** (minimizing overhead for simple tasks), **Enhanced Accuracy** (guaranteeing high precision for complex queries), and **Flexibility**, which permits the integration of domain-specific tools or external APIs.

**4. Agentic Document Workflows (ADW)**
ADW systems represent a significant leap towards end-to-end knowledge work automation, extending the capabilities of both RAG and Intelligent Document Processing (IDP). Unlike traditional RAG, ADW excels at maintaining system state and coordinating complex, multi-step document-centric workflows. The typical ADW workflow follows five structured stages: 

1. **Document Parsing and Information Structuring:** Enterprise-grade tools (e.g., LlamaParse) parse raw documents to extract specific data fields, such as invoice numbers, dates, vendor information, line items, and payment terms. This structured data is then organized for subsequent processing.
2. **State Maintenance Across Processes:** The system tracks document context throughout the workflow, ensuring consistency and relevance across multiple stages. It also monitors the progression of the document through various processing steps.
3. **Knowledge Retrieval:** Relevant references are fetched from external knowledge bases (e.g., LlamaCloud) or vector indexes, and real-time, domain-specific guidelines are retrieved to inform decision-making. 
4. **Agentic Orchestration:** Intelligent agents apply specific business rules and perform multi-hop reasoning. These agents orchestrate components, including parsers, retrievers, and external APIs, generating actionable recommendations.
5. **Actionable Output Generation:** Outputs are structured for specific use cases, synthesizing recommendations and extracted insights into concise reports.

An example highlights this: processing an invoice (INV-2025-045 for $15,000.00) involves parsing it, retrieving associated vendor contract terms to verify payment terms and identify discounts, and finally generating a recommendation report. This report might detail that a 2% early payment discount is available if paid by 2025-04-10, reducing the amount due to $14,700.00, while also noting a 5% bulk order discount because the subtotal exceeded $10,000.00.

### **Synthesis of Frameworks and Capabilities**

Collectively, these advanced architectures exhibit complementary strengths: Agent-G and GeAR excel in integrating structured (graph) and unstructured data for deep relational reasoning; Adaptive RAG provides the meta-level control by optimizing the retrieval strategy based on query complexity; and ADW focuses on the end-to-end automation and stateful execution of business processes using specialized tools and domain rules.

**Operational Insights and Trade-offs:**

1. **Comparative Analysis:** Traditional RAG is limited to isolated retrieval and generation. Agentic RAG improves upon this via multi-agent collaboration and reasoning. ADW takes this further by focusing on deep, document-centric, end-to-end workflows, maintaining state across multi-step processes, and deeply embedding domain-specific business rules and tools. ADW is particularly suited for multi-domain enterprise tasks like contract review, invoice processing, and claims analysis, although it can incur resource overhead and requires initial domain standardization. The inherent complexity means that single-agent architectures favor simplicity, while multi-agent systems improve scalability through parallelism but introduce coordination overhead. Hierarchical and corrective architectures boost reliability via strategic oversight but increase latency. Graph-based systems enable structured relational reasoning but depend heavily on knowledge quality.

2. **Design Principles and Guidance:** Practitioners must understand that Agentic RAG is not a universal default; for simple, well-scoped queries, modular RAG pipelines are sufficient and less resource-intensive. When deploying these systems, constraints are crucial: autonomy must be balanced with predefined tool access policies and explicit stopping criteria to prevent excessive tool invocation or misalignment. The primary bottleneck remains **Retrieval Quality**; no amount of agentic reasoning can compensate for poor indexing or inadequate retrieval coverage. Furthermore, evaluation must move beyond mere output quality, requiring **Process-Level Metrics** that assess reasoning efficiency, tool usage patterns, and adaptation to changing contexts.

3. **The Role of Tools and Ecosystems:**

*   **Multi-Agent Orchestration:** Frameworks such as CrewAI, AutoGen (formerly AG2), and OpenAI Swarm Framework support sophisticated multi-agent collaboration, advanced tool execution, and hierarchical/sequential processes. 
*   **Graph and Vector Integration:** Neo4j, an open-source graph database, excels at handling complex relationships and semantic queries, often paired with vector databases like Weaviate, Pinecone, Milvus, and Qdrant, to form the backbone of high-performance RAG. 
*   **Platforms and APIs:** Industry-leading platforms facilitate deployment. Google's Vertex AI provides scalable integration for contextual awareness. Microsoft's Semantic Kernel is an open-source SDK supporting agentic patterns for task automation. Amazon Bedrock and IBM's watsonx.ai also provide robust platforms for building and deploying such agentic workflows, with models like the Granite-3-8B-Instruct being utilized for complex query resolution.

**Practical Application Domains:**

These systems demonstrate transformative potential across various sectors. In Customer Support, Agentic RAG can generate detailed ad proposals by retrieving advertiser data, historical campaign performance, and audience demographics. For Healthcare, systems integrate patient-specific data (e.g., EHRs) and up-to-date medical literature to produce personalized care recommendations. Legal and Contract Analysis utilize semantic search and legal knowledge graphs to identify critical clauses and potential risks. In Finance, they process real-time data streams and predictive modeling, such as for auto insurance claims, combining policy details with accident data while ensuring regulatory compliance. The need for **Explainability, Traceability, and Auditability** must be treated as first-class design goals in high-stakes applications, necessitating human oversight and clear governance mechanisms.
