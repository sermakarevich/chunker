Large Language Models (LLMs), such as GPT-5, PaLM, and LLaMA, have profoundly transformed artificial intelligence by enabling human-like text generation and natural language understanding. These models facilitate innovations across conversational agents, content creation, real-time translation, and multimodal generation tasks, including text-to-image and text-to-video synthesis. However, a significant challenge remains for LLMs: reliably reasoning over rapidly changing external knowledge rather than solely depending on static pre-training data. Despite these advances, LLMs remain limited by static pre-training data, often resulting in outdated responses, hallucinated content, and an inability to adapt to evolving real-world information.

To address these limitations, Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) emerged. RAG improves factual grounding, relevance, and timeliness by dynamically integrating external knowledge into the generation process at inference time. By combining LLM capabilities with retrieval mechanisms over vector databases, APIs, or web sources, RAG addresses the issues of static data. Although traditional RAG pipelines are typically static and linear, which limits complex multi-step reasoning, deep contextual understanding, and iterative response refinement, the field has continued to evolve. This evolution necessitated autonomous control mechanisms that can dynamically decide when to retrieve, how to reformulate queries, and when sufficient evidence has been collected.

In parallel, advances in agentic AI introduced intelligent agents capable of perception, reasoning, and autonomous task execution. These agents utilize design patterns—specifically reflection, planning, tool use, and multi-agent collaboration—to dynamically decompose tasks and adapt strategies. Since retrieval and reasoning decisions can themselves be treated as agentic tasks, these agentic capabilities are directly relevant to RAG. Furthermore, these agentic patterns are formalized through workflow patterns, such as prompt chaining, routing, parallelization, orchestrator–worker models, and evaluator–optimizer loops, which govern how multiple prompts, models, or agents are coordinated at the system level.

The convergence of RAG and agentic intelligence gives rise to Agentic Retrieval-Augmented Generation (Agentic RAG). Agentic RAG integrates autonomous agents directly into the RAG pipeline. This integration enables dynamic retrieval, iterative context refinement, and adaptive workflow orchestration. This explicit control layer guides how the system reasons over external evidence by coordinating retrieval, validating information, invoking tools, and refining responses. This makes Agentic RAG effective for complex, multi-domain tasks requiring both precision and adaptability.

Despite growing interest, the Agentic RAG field currently lacks a unified taxonomy, remaining fragmented with diverse architectures and inconsistent terminology. Existing work typically focuses on individual frameworks or application-specific implementations. Therefore, this survey proposes structuring the emerging field by reviewing the evolution of RAG paradigms, including naïve, modular, and graph-based RAG, and their transition toward agentic systems. The survey introduces a principled taxonomy of Agentic RAG architectures based on four design dimensions: agent cardinality, control structure, autonomy, and knowledge representation, followed by a comparative analysis of design trade-offs.

Detailed analysis of RAG evolution includes four key paradigms: 
1. **Naïve RAG**: This foundational approach uses simple keyword-based retrieval techniques, such as TF-IDF and BM25, to fetch documents from static datasets. It is simple for fact-based queries but suffers from a lack of contextual awareness (relying only on lexical matching), often resulting in fragmented or overly generic outputs, and struggling with scalability on large datasets.
2. **Advanced RAG**: Building on Naïve RAG, Advanced RAG incorporates semantic understanding and enhanced retrieval techniques. These systems utilize dense retrieval models, such as Dense Passage Retrieval (DPR), and neural ranking algorithms to enhance retrieval precision. Key features include: **Dense Vector Search**, which represents queries and documents in high-dimensional vector spaces for better semantic alignment; **Contextual Re-Ranking**, where neural models re-rank documents to prioritize relevant information; and **Iterative Retrieval**, which enables reasoning across multiple documents for complex queries. Advanced RAG is suitable for high-precision tasks like research synthesis but still faces computational overhead and limited scalability challenges with massive datasets.
3. **Modular RAG**: This paradigm emphasizes flexibility and customization by decomposing the retrieval and generation pipeline into independent, reusable components. Key innovations include: **Hybrid Retrieval Strategies**, which combine sparse methods (e.g., sparse encoder-BM25) with dense techniques (e.g., DPR) to maximize accuracy for diverse query types; **Tool Integration**, allowing the system to use external APIs, databases, or computational tools for specialized tasks (like real-time data analysis); and **Composable Pipelines**, enabling components (like retrievers and generators) to be replaced or enhanced independently. Modular RAG is ideal for complex, multi-domain tasks, offering both scalability and precision.
4. **Graph RAG**: Graph RAG extends traditional RAG systems by integrating graph-based data structures, allowing for more complex relationship modeling.

Practical application domains for Agentic RAG reviewed in this paper include healthcare, finance, and education.
