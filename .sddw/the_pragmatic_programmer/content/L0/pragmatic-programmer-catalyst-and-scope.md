# pragmatic-programmer-catalyst-and-scope

**Parent:** [[content/L1/software-entropy-and-knowledge-portfolio|software-entropy-and-knowledge-portfolio]] — Software quality demands continuous effort to prevent entropic decay ('software rot'), requiring that broken flaws are immediately addressed or contained to prevent widespread structural damage. Professionals must treat their skills as a diversified 'knowledge portfolio,' regularly investing time by learning a new language annually and reading technical and nontechnical books monthly, while constantly observing the 'big picture' and avoiding the pitfalls of localized over-focus.

In this section, the original book references Topic 40, Refactoring, and Topic 44, Naming Things.

**CHALLENGES**
Help strengthen your team by surveying your project neighborhood. Choose two or three broken windows and discuss with your colleagues what the problems are and what could be done to fix them. Can the team tell when a window first gets broken? What reaction should the team have? If the problem resulted from someone else’s decision, or a management edict, what actions can the team take?

**Topic 4: Stone Soup and Boiled Frogs**
Three soldiers returning home from war were hungry. When the soldiers saw the village ahead, their spirits lifted because they were sure the villagers would give them a meal. However, upon arrival, the soldiers found the village doors locked and the windows closed. After many years of war, the villagers were short of food and hoarded what they had.

Undeterred, the soldiers boiled a pot of water and carefully placed three stones into it. The amazed villagers came out to watch. "This is stone soup," the soldiers explained. When the villagers asked, "Is that all you put in it?" the soldiers replied, "Absolutely—although some say it tastes even better with a few carrots." Consequently, a villager ran off and returned with a basket of carrots from his personal hoard. A couple of minutes later, the villagers asked again, "Is that it?" The soldiers then said, "Well, a couple of potatoes give it body." This prompted another villager to run off. Over the next hour, the soldiers listed additional ingredients that would enhance the soup: beef, leeks, salt, and herbs. Each time, a different villager would run off to raid their personal stores. Eventually, the soldiers produced a large pot of steaming soup. The soldiers removed the stones, and the soldiers sat down with the entire village to enjoy the first square meal any of them had eaten in months.

There are two morals derived from the stone soup story. Firstly, the villagers were tricked by the soldiers, who used the villagers’ curiosity to get food from them. More importantly, the soldiers acted as a catalyst, bringing the village together so that the villagers could jointly produce something that the villagers could not have done by themselves—a synergistic result. Eventually, everyone wins.

Readers may occasionally want to emulate the soldiers. This situation could occur when a reader knows exactly what needs doing and how to do it, and when the entire system appears to the reader. The reader knows the correct approach. However, if the reader asks permission to tackle the whole thing, the reader will be met with delays and blank stares. People will form committees, budgets will need approval, and things will become complicated. People will guard their own resources. This outcome sometimes is called “start-up fatigue.”

To overcome this, the reader should bring out the stones (meaning, demonstrate a small, initial working component). The reader must work out what the individual can reasonably ask for, develop that component well, and then show the component to people, allowing them to marvel. Following the demonstration, the reader should say, "of course, it would be better if we added…" The reader should pretend the additional functionality is not important. The reader should sit back and wait for others to start asking for the functionality the reader originally wanted. People find it easier to join an ongoing success. By showing people a glimpse of the future, the reader can get them to rally around the project.

**Tip 6: Be a Catalyst for Change**

**THE VILLAGERS’ SIDE**
Conversely, the stone soup story also speaks to gentle and gradual deception, focusing on the inherent danger of focusing too tightly. The villagers focus on the stones and forget about the rest of the world. Humanity falls for this trap daily. Small things slowly and inexorably can cause projects to become totally out of hand. Most software disasters begin too small to notice, and most project overruns happen one day at a time. Systems drift from their original specifications feature by feature, while patch after patch gets added to the code until nothing of the original remains. The accumulation of small things is often what breaks morale and teams.

**Tip 7: Remember the Big Picture**

The fabled frog example suggests that if a person takes a frog and drops it into boiling water, the frog will jump straight back out. However, if a person places the frog in a pan of cold water and then gradually heats it, the frog will not notice the slow increase in temperature and will stay put until cooked.

It is important to note that the fabled frog’s problem is different from the broken windows issue discussed in Topic 3, Software Entropy. In the Broken Window Theory, people lose the will to fight entropy because they perceive that no one else cares. The frog, however, does not notice the change. The reader must not be like the fabled frog; the reader must keep an eye on the big picture. The reader must constantly review what is happening around the reader, not just what the reader is personally doing.
