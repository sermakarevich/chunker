# blackboard-architecture-patterns

**Parent:** [[content/L1/professional-toolkit-and-distributed-concurrency|professional-toolkit-and-distributed-concurrency]] — Professional mastery requires mastering a comprehensive toolkit, covering physical instruments, systematic workflow management (using techniques like PERT/Scenario-Based and Iterative Estimation), and advanced programming skills (including shell command mastery, editor fluency, and VCS management). For concurrency, developers use the Actor Model (independent virtual processors with private state and unidirectional message passing) or Blackboard systems (central, stateful coordination mechanisms suitable for complex, asynchronous data gathering), while debugging relies on structured techniques like the 'chop' method and the use of tracing statements.

Blackboards are coordination mechanisms suitable for solving large and complex problems where the solution is not immediately obvious. The board is conceptually used by independent agents, such as detectives solving a murder investigation, to accumulate and organize scattered information. For instance, the chief inspector might start by writing a single question, like 'H. Dumpty (Male, Egg): Accident? Murder? Did Humpty really fall, or was he pushed?' The detectives then contribute facts, witness statements, forensic evidence, or observations to this potential murder mystery. As data accumulates, a detective may notice a connection and post that observation or speculation. This continuous process involves many different people and agents across multiple shifts until the case is closed.

Key characteristics of the blackboard approach include: The detectives need not know the existence of other detectives; instead, each detective simply observes the board for new information and adds findings. The participating detectives may be trained in different disciplines, possess different levels of education and expertise, and may not even work in the same precinct. While the detectives share a desire to solve the case, they may not otherwise interact. Additionally, different detectives may come and go during the course of the process, working different shifts. There are no restrictions on what type of information can be placed on the blackboard, which may include pictures, sentences, or physical evidence.

This model represents a form of laissez faire concurrency. Independent processes, agents, or actors store facts on the blackboard, while other processes take facts off the board—sometimes combining or processing them—and then post new information back to the board. Gradually, the board guides the system toward a conclusion.

Computer-based blackboard systems were originally used in artificial intelligence applications, solving complex problems such as speech recognition and knowledge-based reasoning systems. One of the first blackboard systems was David Gelernter’s Linda, which stored facts as typed tuples. Applications could write new tuples into Linda and query for existing tuples using a form of pattern matching. Later systems included distributed blackboard-like systems such as JavaSpaces and T Spaces. These advanced systems allow storing active Java objects—not just data—on the blackboard, enabling retrieval by partial matching of fields (using templates and wildcards) or by subtypes. For example, a system could search a blackboard containing `Person` objects using an `Author` template and a specific `lastName` value, retrieving Bill Shakespeare the author, but not Fred Shakespeare the gardener.

Blackboard systems are particularly well-suited for complex data gathering tasks, such as processing mortgage or loan applications. Given the odiously complex legal requirements, which involve federal, state, and local governments, and the potential for receiving responses in any order (for example, a credit check may take a substantial amount of time while name and address may be available immediately), a blackboard is an elegant solution. Because the blackboard is used, the order of data arrival is irrelevant: when a fact is posted, it can trigger the appropriate rules. Feedback is also easily handled, as the output of any set of rules can post to the blackboard and cause the triggering of yet more applicable rules. This approach is contrasted with complex workflow systems that require constant reorganization and hard-wired code rewriting as regulations change.

In modern architectures, messaging systems like Kafka and NATS can function similarly to blackboards. These systems offer more than simple data transfer from A to B; they provide persistence (in the form of an event log) and the ability to retrieve messages through pattern matching, enabling their use as both a blackboard system and a platform for actors. While adopting the actor or blackboard approach removes a whole class of potential concurrency problems, this benefit introduces complexity: the action becomes indirect and harder to reason about. To manage this complexity, developers should maintain a central repository of message formats and/or APIs (especially one that generates code and documentation) and utilize good tooling to trace messages and facts as they progress. A useful technique includes adding a unique trace ID when a business function starts and then propagating that ID to all involved actors to reconstruct the full sequence of events from the log files. This approach helps manage the challenges related to increased moving parts and deployment complexity, though the system benefits from the increased granularity by allowing replacement of individual actors rather than the entire system.
