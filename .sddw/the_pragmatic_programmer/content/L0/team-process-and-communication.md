# team-process-and-communication

**Parent:** [[content/L1/adaptive-development-and-collaboration|adaptive-development-and-collaboration]] — Effective development mandates continuous adaptation through a three-step feedback loop (status identification, smallest meaningful step, evaluation/fixing), and requires collaborative methods like Pair or Mob Programming, which should be applied across all roles and ideally include users. Technical mastery requires deep fluency in tools (e.g., command shells, editors), mastering estimation via PERT/Iterative methods, and adopting advanced architectural patterns like PubSub, Reactive Programming, and the Actor Model.

Effective software development requires adopting continuous adaptation, treating the process as an organic activity rather than a static construction effort. Because development is complex, the team must proactively address potential weaknesses by establishing small, stable, and trusting team structures. Teams must take shared responsibility for the product's quality, ensuring they do not tolerate 'broken windows'—small, overlooked imperfections. Teams must also guard against the 'boiled frog' syndrome, where individuals or the team become oblivious to significant environmental changes, such as increased scope, decreased time scales, or additional features, often by assuming others are handling the issue. To combat this, every team member must actively monitor the environment for changes and maintain metrics on new requirements.

Furthermore, teams must deliberately schedule time for continuous improvement and innovation, dedicating time beyond just feature development. This schedule should include dedicated work on old systems maintenance, time for process reflection and refinement (adopting the principle of continuous improvement), dedicated time for vetting new technologies with prototypes, and planning team-wide skill improvements. Scheduling this effort is crucial to ensure that internal improvements are not perpetually deferred.

In terms of communication, while developers must talk to each other (as detailed in the 'Communicate!' topic), the team itself—as an entity—must communicate clearly with the wider organization. The ideal project team should possess a distinct, positive personality, making others look forward to interactions, and should produce documentation that is crisp, accurate, and consistent. One marketing technique to enhance this team identity is to generate a brand: when starting a project, a team should select and use an off-the-wall name and generate a zany logo, using the team's name liberally in conversations. This provides an identity for the team to build upon.

Maintaining efficiency requires resisting duplication of effort, which can lead to 'stovepipe' or 'siloed' systems and wasted maintenance effort. Good communication must be instantaneous and frictionless; team members should be able to answer questions or share status updates quickly, whether by physically poking a coworker's office or using a messaging application. Waiting a week for a team meeting to ask a question creates excessive friction.

Finally, development activities should not be viewed as isolated roles (such as separate specialized teams for design, frontend, server, or QA). Instead, all roles are different views of the same problem. The recommended approach, utilizing the 'tracer bullet' method, involves developing individual features, no matter how small or limited initially, that flow end-to-end through the entire system. This ensures the team possesses all necessary skills (e.g., frontend, UI/UX, server, DBA, QA) comfortable enough to work together, allowing the team to rapidly implement small features, receive immediate feedback, and tune the team and process quickly and easily.
