# solving-impossible-puzzles

**Parent:** [[content/L1/professional-mastery-workflow-architecture|professional-mastery-workflow-architecture]] — Professional mastery requires integrating foundational skills (like woodworker tool maintenance) with advanced workflows, including structured estimation methods (PERT, iterative cycles) and digital proficiencies (command shell mastery, editor fluency, using VCS for branching and CI/CD). Crucially, the development process mandates treating initial client statements as explorations, generating feedback via prototypes to distinguish underlying business policy from simple requirements, and adopting advanced architectural patterns like FSMs and PubSub for low coupling.

When solving complex problems, developers must adopt the principle of identifying real constraints and understanding the available degrees of freedom, as opposed to being guided by preconceived notions. The core challenge is not merely to think outside the box, but rather to identify the actual boundaries of the problem—to find the 'box.' Developers must challenge any preconceived notions and evaluate whether or not they constitute real, hard-and-fast constraints. When faced with an intractable problem, developers should enumerate all possible avenues, even those that sound unusable or stupid, and then go through the list to explain why certain paths cannot be taken, ensuring that the impossibility is proven. Developers should categorize and prioritize constraints, starting by identifying the most restrictive constraints first and fitting the remaining constraints within those bounds. A useful technique is to examine a novel solution, such as a Trojan horse: determining how to get troops into a walled city without being discovered, requiring the challenging of previously dismissed methods like 'through the front door.'

Furthermore, solving difficult projects can benefit from stepping away from the problem. While the conscious brain is aware of the problem, the more associative neural net that lies below consciousness may provide insights. Psychology Today reported that people who were distracted performed better on a complex problem-solving task than people who put in conscious effort. If deliberate distraction is not an option, talking about the problem—a technique known as Rubber Ducking—can facilitate enlightenment by prompting questions such as: What is the benefit of solving this problem? Are the current problems related to edge cases that can be eliminated? Is there a simpler, related problem that can be solved?

In terms of project constraints, developers must ask if all constraints handed to the team at the start of the project are still applicable and if the interpretation of those constraints remains valid.

Historical examples illustrate this principle: Joseph Costello, President of Cadence, recounted an 'impossible' project involving an end-of-life, ancient system that generated 17,000 pages of documentation. This project required developing a brand-new system capable of handling hundreds of millions of dollars in money flow, with a deadline of months from inception to deployment. The project was a roaring success because the expert who had managed the system for years was continuously available for questions, clarifications, decisions, and demonstrations, confirming that working closely with users is essential.
