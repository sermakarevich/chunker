# pragmatic-developers-and-user-delight

**Parent:** [[content/L1/software-professional-practices-and-ethics|software-professional-practices-and-ethics]] — Professional software development requires an ethical commitment to user protection and a focus on solving underlying business problems rather than just delivering features, necessitating full automation, state-coverage testing (like Property-Based Testing), and advanced architectural practices like using the Actor Model or PubSub to maintain decoupling.

A pragmatic approach to software development emphasizes that the core goal is to delight the users, rather than merely generating revenue or forcing users to comply with developer objectives. Delivering working software in a timely manner, while necessary, is insufficient for user delight. Users primarily have a business problem, and the team must focus on solving that specific business problem within the scope of the user's objectives and budget. The user's expectations are often not related to software functionality and may not even be present in the initial specifications, as the specifications will require multiple iterations with the development team to become complete. To uncover these underlying user expectations, the team should ask the guiding question: How will the team know that everyone has been successful one month (or one year, or another duration) after this project concludes? The measure of success is typically the expected business value—such as customer retention (for a product recommendation project) or data quality/cost savings (for consolidating two databases)—not just the successful completion of the software project itself. Once these underlying expectations of business value are surfaced, the team must ensure that every team member is fully clear about these expectations. Furthermore, when making development decisions, the team must think about which path forward moves the project closer to the stated expectations. Teams should critically analyze the user requirements based on the known expectations, recognizing that a stated "requirement" might actually be an amateur implementation plan dressed up as a requirements document, rather than a true need. If the team can demonstrate that a suggested change will move the project closer to the overarching objective, developers should not be afraid to make that suggestion. Maintaining a focus on these underlying expectations must continue as the project progresses, as increasing the team's knowledge of the domain allows the developers to make suggestions on other ways to address the underlying business issues that might not be obvious to individual departments.
