**Topic 7: Communicate!**

As developers, people must recognize that effective communication is vital, as possessing the best ideas, the finest code, or the most pragmatic thinking remains sterile unless communication facilitates sharing with other people. The text notes that a good idea is an orphan without effective communication.

Communication occurs on multiple levels: developers spend hours in meetings listening and talking; developers work with end users to understand their needs; developers write code, which communicates intentions to a machine and documents thinking for future generations of developers; developers write proposals and memos for requesting and justifying resources, reporting status, and suggesting new approaches; and developers daily advocate their ideas, modify existing practices, and suggest new ones within teams. Because a large part of the daily work involves communicating, people must execute this task well. Developers should treat natural languages, including English, as another programming language, writing natural language according to principles like honoring the DRY (Don't Repeat Yourself) principle, utilizing automation, and similar design principles. 

**Tip 11: English is Just Another Programming Language**

Effective communication requires two steps: first, KNOWING YOUR AUDIENCE, and second, KNOWING WHAT TO SAY.

**Know Your Audience:** Communication only occurs when a person successfully conveys intended meaning. To achieve this, a person must understand the needs, interests, and capabilities of the audience. For instance, when presenting an update to change a remote monitoring system to use a third-party message broker for status notifications, the communication pitch differs depending on the audience: end users appreciate that their systems can now interoperate with other services; marketing departments can use this fact to boost sales; and development and operations managers are satisfied because the care and maintenance of that system part becomes someone else's problem. By creating the appropriate pitch for each group, the person can excite all groups regarding the project. Furthermore, the trick in all forms of communication is to gather feedback; a person must not simply wait for questions but must actively ask for them and observe body language and facial expressions.
