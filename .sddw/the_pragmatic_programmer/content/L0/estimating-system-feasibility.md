# estimating-system-feasibility

**Parent:** [[content/L1/software-estimation-and-domain-modeling-principles|software-estimation-and-domain-modeling-principles]] — Software design requires prioritizing the domain's vocabulary (advanced practitioners may use internal languages like RSpec) over inherent programming language features (e.g., static vs. dynamic typing). Estimation involves establishing a model (through consulting experts or creating mental models) and decomposing it, ensuring the final answer is expressed in terms of critical, varying parameters, and all estimates require rigorous logging and must be communicated with the phrase, 'I’ll get back to you.'

Regarding software development, a concept called 'Estimating' involves calculating potential time, storage, or performance metrics, which is a valuable skill that helps a professional understand the feasibility and scope of the system's domain.

When determining an estimate, the first step is to understand the context in which the answer will be used, asking whether a high degree of accuracy or a rough ballpark figure is needed. The units chosen for the estimate are critical, as stating a duration of '130 working days' implies a higher degree of accuracy than saying 'about six months,' even if both represent the same total duration. To appropriately scale time estimates, the following guidelines should be followed: 1–15 days should be quoted in Days; 3–6 weeks should be quoted in Weeks; 8–20 weeks should be quoted in Months; and 20+ weeks should be quoted in Months. Therefore, if a project is estimated to take 125 working days (which equals 25 weeks), the professional should communicate this estimate as 'about six months.'

All estimates are fundamentally based on models of the problem. Before developing complex models, a basic and highly effective estimating trick is to consult someone who has previously solved a similar problem. While an exact match for the problem is unlikely, others' experiences can be drawn upon successfully.

The initial phase of any estimation exercise requires building an understanding of the domain and the precise scope of the question. The stated scope often becomes part of the answer; for example, one might estimate reaching a destination in 20 minutes by assuming 'there are no traffic accidents and there’s gas in the car.'

To build a model of the system, the professional should create a rough-and-ready, bare-bones mental model based on the understanding of the question. This model might involve conceptualizing a server and arriving traffic if response times are being estimated, or mapping out the steps an organization uses during the development process for a project.

Building the model is beneficial because it often leads to discoveries of underlying patterns and processes that were not apparent on the surface. The process of building the model allows the professional to reexamine the original question, potentially realizing that 'Y, a variant of X, could be done in about half the time, and you lose only one feature.'

Once the model is established, the professional must decompose it into smaller components. The core task at this stage is discovering the mathematical rules that govern how these components interact. Sometimes a component contributes a single added value, while other components may supply multiplicative factors, and some components may be more complex, such as those simulating the arrival of traffic at a node.

For each component, the professional needs to identify its parameters. Next, the professional must assign a specific value to each parameter, focusing particularly on the parameters that have the most significant impact on the result. The parameters whose values are multiplied or divided are typically more significant than those that are simply added to a result. The professional should develop a justifiable method for calculating these critical parameters, such as measuring the actual transaction arrival rate of an existing system or finding a similar system for measurement. Since the process often involves basing one estimate on others, this is where the largest potential errors can occur.

After completing the calculations, the professional should recognize that complex systems rarely yield a single answer. Instead, one should run multiple calculations, varying the values of the critical parameters, until the ones that truly drive the model are identified. Using a spreadsheet can assist this process. The final answer should be expressed in terms of these identified parameters; for instance, stating that 'The response time is roughly three quarters of a second if the system has SSDs and 32GB of memory, and one second with 16GB memory' conveys a different sense of accuracy than simply stating '750ms.' If the arithmetic is correct, but the resulting answer seems strange, the professional should deduce that the understanding of the problem or the model used is probably flawed, which is valuable information.
