# avoiding-cargo-cult-traps-in-development

**Parent:** [[content/L1/software-development-methodology-synthesis|software-development-methodology-synthesis]] — Effective software development mandates full automation of build, test, and deployment procedures (CI/CD), utilizing Version Control to trigger processes via commits and ensuring roles are not siloed through the 'tracer bullet' method. Teams must adopt systematic estimation techniques like PERT (optimistic/most likely/pessimistic figures) and Iterative Estimation, focusing on delivering working software on demand while resisting the 'cargo cult' trap.

When adopting software development practices, teams must avoid falling into the 'cargo cult' trap: building easily-visible artifacts or superficial structures (such as making a fake airport out of coconut shells) hoping to attract the underlying, working magic.

Teams should question why they use a particular development method, framework, or testing technique, asking if it is genuinely suited for the specific job or if it was adopted only because a successful company was using it. While companies like Spotify, Netflix, Stripe, and GitLab have unique approaches to software development and management, teams must consider their specific context, including whether they operate in the same market, face similar constraints and opportunities, possess similar expertise and organizational size, management, culture, and user base and requirements.

Particular artifacts, superficial structures, policies, processes, and methods alone are insufficient for success. Instead, teams must focus on adopting what genuinely works by piloting the idea with a small team or set of teams, keeping the elements that seem effective, and discarding anything else as waste or overhead. Years from now, successful companies will continue to mature, pivot, and adopt different processes.

Since the purpose of a software development methodology is to help people work together, teams must remember that there is no single plan for developing software, nor is a plan created by another company necessarily applicable. Instead, teams should take the best pieces from any particular methodology and adapt them, recognizing that no single method fits all. For example, Scrum defines some project management practices, but it does not provide enough guidance for technical teams or for leadership at the portfolio/governance level.

The fundamental goal of software development is to deliver working software that gives the users new capabilities at a moment's notice, rather than weeks, months, or years from now. If a team is currently delivering in years, it should aim to shorten the cycle to months; from months, cut it to weeks; from a four-week sprint, attempt two weeks; from a two-week sprint, aim for one week; and finally, on demand. Delivering on demand means delivering when the users need it and when it makes business sense to do so.
