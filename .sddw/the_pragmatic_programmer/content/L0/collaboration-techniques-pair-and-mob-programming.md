# collaboration-techniques-pair-and-mob-programming

**Parent:** [[index]]

Effective collaboration in software development requires combining discussion with question-asking and active coding, which is significantly more powerful than merely discussing problems or taking notes. Conway's Law, introduced by Melvin Conway in 1967, dictates that organizations designing systems produce designs that mirror the communication structures of the organization. For instance, a team lacking communication may result in siloed, 'stove-pipe' systems, while teams divided into two may produce a client/server or frontend/backend division. Furthermore, the developer should consider applying these principles of collaboration to technical practices. When one developer operates the keyboard (the typist) and the second developer does not, both developers work on the problem together and can switch typing duties as needed. This practice, known as Pair Programming, benefits from the fact that different people contribute varied backgrounds, experiences, problem-solving techniques, approaches, and levels of focus. The developer acting as typist must focus on low-level syntax and coding style details, allowing the second developer to focus on higher-level issues and scope. Because human brain bandwidth is limited, having a second developer's full attention available during the task significantly increases mental power, and the inherent peer-pressure from the second person helps prevent momentary lapses or poor naming habits, thereby elevating software quality. An advanced form of this collaboration is Mob Programming, which involves more than two developers collaborating on the same problem simultaneously, utilizing one typist. Proponents report that mobs can solve hard problems effectively and can easily include people outside the core development team, such as users, project sponsors, and testers. For example, a small mob of three consisting of one developer typing while another discussed the issue with a business expert was observed during one project. Developers should try Pair Programming if currently working solo, dedicating a minimum of two weeks for a few hours at a time, as this method may initially feel strange. For brainstorming new ideas or diagnosing complex issues, trying a Mob Programming session may prove beneficial. If a team is already pairing or mobbing, team members should consider extending participation beyond just developers to include users, testers, or sponsors. When managing collaboration, it is critical to focus on building the code, not the ego, starting small (for example, a mob with only 4-5 people, or starting with just a few pairs in short sessions), criticizing the code, not the person (for example, stating 'Let’s look at this block,' instead of 'you’re wrong'), and actively listening to and trying to understand others’ viewpoints, recognizing that 'different isn’t wrong.' The team should also conduct frequent retrospectives to improve future efforts. In conclusion, coding in any setting—whether in the same office or remotely, alone, in pairs, or in mobs—constitutes an effective way to solve problems. If a team is accustomed to one style, the team might experiment with a different one, but developers must research the rules, suggestions, and guidelines for each style. Finally, developers must remember that agility is an adjective describing *how* one performs tasks (for example, 'an agile developer' or 'a team that responds to change with agility'), not a noun describing a person or process. The core values highlighted by the Agile Manifesto are: valuing individuals and interactions over processes and tools, working software over comprehensive documentation, customer collaboration over contract negotiation, and responding to change over following a plan.
