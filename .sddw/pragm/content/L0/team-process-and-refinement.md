# team-process-and-refinement

**Parent:** [[content/L1/software-development-architecture-best-practices|software-development-architecture-best-practices]] — Successful software development requires an adaptive, context-specific approach guided by continuous feedback loops, utilizing principles like DRY, orthogonality, and least privilege to manage architectural complexity. Teams must facilitate communication instantly (using 'tracer bullets' to ensure all skills traverse the whole system end-to-end), automate all processes, and adhere to stringent security practices such as encrypting all sensitive data, utilizing third-party authentication, and never committing credentials alongside source code.

To achieve continuous improvement, teams must dedicate time to reviewing what mechanisms are effective and which are failing, and then actively making changes. Teams should schedule this process; fixing problems requires dedicated time.

When adopting new technologies, frameworks, or libraries, teams must avoid simply implementing them because "everyone is doing it," or because of content seen at conferences or online. Instead, teams should deliberately vet candidate technologies by developing prototypes and scheduling time to analyze the results.

For skill development, personal learning is beneficial, but spreading skills across the entire team is more effective. Teams should plan for knowledge sharing, whether through informal brown-bag lunches or formal training sessions.

Regarding team presence and communication, the team itself must communicate clearly with the outside world. For outsiders, the most detrimental project teams appear sullen and reticent, holding meetings with no structure, and producing messy project documents with inconsistent terminology. Highly successful project teams, conversely, develop a distinct personality, providing well-prepared, positive meetings and producing documentation that is crisp, accurate, and consistent. To help a team communicate as a unified entity, a simple marketing strategy is to generate a brand: teams should create a memorable, off-the-wall name for the project and use it liberally when communicating with people, providing an identity for the world to associate with the work.

To prevent duplication of effort among team members, communication must be instant and frictionless. If the team is physically co-located, this means simple physical proximity; for remote teams, reliable messaging apps or electronic means are necessary. Waiting a week for a team meeting to ask a question or share a status creates significant friction, and maintaining a frictionless flow of information regarding progress, problems, insights, and learnings is crucial for staying DRY (Don't Repeat Yourself).

Since a project team must complete many different tasks across various technologies—including understanding requirements, designing architecture, coding for frontend and server, and testing—it is a common misconception that these activities can happen separately and in isolation. This is incorrect because all activities are different views of the same core problem. Artificially separating roles or creating specialized teams introduces 'gates' and 'handoffs,' which interrupt the smooth flow from the team to deployment. These unnecessary checkpoints and approvals are considered waste according to Lean principles. Programmers who are two or three levels removed from the actual users of the code are often unable to make informed decisions regarding the context of the work. Therefore, the recommended approach is the 'tracer bullets' method: developing individual features, no matter how small, that traverse the entire system end-to-end. This requires the team to possess all necessary skills—such as frontend, UI/UX, server, DBA, and QA—and be accustomed to collaborating. Using a tracer bullet approach allows the team to implement small functionality quickly and receive immediate feedback on the quality of team communication and delivery.

To ensure consistency and accuracy, teams should automate all aspects of the project development and production deployment. Automation should cover tasks like code formatting, running automated tests during continuous builds, and deploying the system reliably and repeatedly. Teams must incorporate the skills necessary to build and deploy these automation tools. Finally, although teams are composed of individuals and should each be given the ability to shine in their own way, giving the team just enough structure to support the project's value, developers must resist the temptation to add excessive features or complexities to the product.
