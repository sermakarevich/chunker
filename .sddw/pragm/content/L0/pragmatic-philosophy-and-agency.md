# pragmatic-philosophy-and-agency

**Parent:** [[content/L1/pragmatic-programmer-philosophy-and-accountability|pragmatic-programmer-philosophy-and-accountability]] — Pragmatic programming requires developers to take full ownership of their careers and projects, emphasizing continuous improvement (Kaizen), managing technical entropy, and maintaining transparency in failures by admitting errors immediately and providing solutions.

Beginning by discussing strategies for change is important, especially because inertia often makes change difficult. In Topic 4, Stone Soup and Boiled Frogs, the book examines a strategy for instigating change and presents the cautionary tale of an amphibian that ignored the dangers of gradual change. Understanding the context in which a person works benefits a person because it makes knowing the required quality level of software easier. While near-perfection might be the only option, Topic 5, Good-Enough Software, explores involved trade-offs. Furthermore, a person needs a broad base of knowledge and experience to execute all of the concepts discussed. Learning is a continuous and ongoing process, and Topic 6, Your Knowledge Portfolio, discusses strategies for maintaining momentum. Finally, because no person works in a vacuum, and all people spend a large amount of time interacting with others, Topic 7, Communicate!, lists methods for improving communication. These discussions collectively establish the basis for pragmatic thinking, the philosophy underpinning pragmatic programming.

Topic 1: It’s Your Life

This section begins with the affirmation: "It is your life. You own it. You run it. You create it." Many developers express frustration with varied concerns: some feel stagnant in their job, some feel that technology has passed them by, others feel undervalued, underpaid, or that their teams are toxic. Potential desired changes include moving to Asia, Europe, or working from home. The consistent answer provided by the book is: "Why can’t you change it?"
