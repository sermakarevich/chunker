# communication-pragmatic-skills

**Parent:** [[content/L1/knowledge-portfolio-and-communication|knowledge-portfolio-and-communication]] — The programmer's knowledge portfolio must be managed through continuous investment, diversification across unrelated subjects, and systematic review and rebalancing of skills. This includes annual commitment to learning new programming languages (such as Clojure, Elixir, Elm, F#, Go, Haskell, Python, R, ReasonML, Ruby, Rust, Scala, Swift, TypeScript, etc.) and dedicating time to reading one technical or nontechnical book monthly to ensure comprehensive expertise and adaptive capability.

To analyze problems, consider structured thinking processes. A popular consulting method involves asking “why?” repeatedly, ideally at least five times. By asking a question and receiving an answer, one can dig deeper by asking “why?” and repeating this process, similar to how a polite, petulant four-year-old would ask. Following this process might help determine a root cause. Furthermore, one can analyze situations by following the money, recognizing that the benefits to an individual or to another organization may align with personal goals or not.

When considering a situation, one must understand the context, because the statement that "one size fits all" solutions are effective is often incorrect. When considering an article or book that promotes a "best practice," a person should ask the following questions: to whom is this practice best? What are the prerequisites and the potential consequences, both short-term and long-term?

Analyzing the timing is also critical. One must determine under what circumstances a solution will work, and whether the solution is too late or too early. Regarding the process, one should not stop at first-order thinking (predicting what will happen next); instead, one must use second-order thinking to consider what will happen after the first consequence. Finally, one should investigate the problem by determining if there is an underlying model and how that model functions.

Although simple answers are rare today, an individual can understand complex answers by maintaining an extensive knowledge portfolio and applying critical analysis to the diverse stream of technical articles that the individual reads.

**Challenges for Continuous Learning and Professional Development:**
*   **Language Skills:** Learners should start mastering a new programming language this week. If a developer is always programmed in the same language, the learner should try languages such as Clojure, Elixir, Elm, F#, Go, Haskell, Python, R, ReasonML, Ruby, Rust, Scala, Swift, TypeScript, or any other language that appeals or looks suitable.
*   **Reading Material:** Learners should start reading a new technical book, but the reader must finish the current book first. If the individual is engaged in very detailed implementation and coding, the learner should read a book focused on design and architecture. If the individual is involved in high-level design, the learner should read a book concerning coding techniques.
*   **Networking:** Learners should actively speak about technology with people who are not involved in the individual's current project or who do not work for the same company. Possible places to do this include the company cafeteria or local meetups with fellow enthusiasts.

**Topic 7: Communicate!**
*   *Lesson:* Mae West, Belle of the Nineties, said, “It is not just what you’ve got, but also how you package it.” This suggests that possessing excellent ideas, fine code, or the most pragmatic thinking is useless unless one can communicate these concepts to other people. A strong idea, if not communicated effectively, is like an orphan.
*   **Communication Skills:** As developers, individuals must communicate on multiple levels: communicating during hours spent in meetings while listening and talking; working with end users to understand their needs; writing code, which communicates intentions to a machine and documents thought processes for future developers; writing proposals and memos to request and justify resources, report status, and suggest new approaches; and participating in daily team work to advocate ideas, modify existing practices, and suggest new ones. Because a large part of daily life involves communicating, developers must improve their ability to do so well.
*   **Applying DRY to Language:** Developers should treat English (or the person's native language) as just another programming language. One must write natural language following principles like the DRY (Don't Repeat Yourself) principle, automation, and other design principles (the text notes that a full discussion of DRY and ETC design principles occurs in the next chapter).
*   **Principle 1: Know Your Audience:** Effective communication requires conveying exactly what one means to convey; merely talking is insufficient. To communicate effectively, one must understand the needs, interests, and capabilities of the specific audience. For example, presenting an update about changing a remote monitoring system to use a third-party message broker to disseminate status notifications can be pitched differently depending on the audience: End users will appreciate the interoperation with other services; the marketing department can use this fact to boost sales; and development and operations managers will be happy because the care and maintenance of that part of the system will become someone else’s responsibility. When communicating, the individual must remember the trick is to gather feedback, which means asking for questions instead of just waiting for them, and observing body language and facial expressions. The text notes that one Neuro Linguistic Programming presupposition is that "The meaning of your communication is the response you get." The individual should continuously improve knowledge of the audience while communicating.
*   **Principle 2: Know What You Want to Say:** The most difficult part of formal business communication is determining exactly what must be conveyed. While fiction writers often plot their books in detail before starting, individuals writing technical documents often begin by simply typing an outline, such as "1. Introduction," and continue writing whatever comes to mind. To write effectively, one must first plan what one wants to say, write an outline, and then ask whether the communication conveys the intended message to the audience in a usable manner. This planning approach should also be used for important meetings or chats with major clients, where one should jot down ideas and plan several strategies for delivering them.
*   **Principle 3: Choose Your Moment:** One must consider the audience's needs and priorities to know what they need to hear. For example, if an individual catches a manager who was recently criticized by a boss because some source code was lost, the manager will be a more receptive listener for ideas concerning source code repositories. To ensure relevance, the message must be relevant both in time and in content. Whenever possible, one should ask the simple question, “Is this a good time to talk about…?”
*   **Principle 4: Choose a Style:** The individual must adjust the style of delivery to suit the audience. Some people prefer a formal “just the facts’’ briefing, while others prefer a long, wide-ranging discussion before addressing the core business. The individual must assess the audience's skill level and experience in the area to determine if the audience members are experts, novices, or if they require hand-holding or just a quick summary (tl;dr). If uncertain, one should ask. Importantly, the individual must remember that the person is only half of the communication transaction; if someone asks for a paragraph description but the individual cannot see a way of doing it in less than several pages, the individual must tell them so, as this type of feedback is also a form of communication.
*   **Principle 5: Make It Look Good:** The visual presentation of ideas is crucial. Although many developers and their managers focus only on content when producing written documents, this is considered a mistake. Any chef (or watcher of the Food Network) can demonstrate that hours of work in the kitchen can be ruined by poor presentation. For modern printed documents, there is no excuse for poor appearance. While the document may be written using Markdown or a word processor, the creator must learn basic commands and use style sheets for consistency (the company may already have defined style sheets available). Developers should learn to set page headers and footers, use included sample documents for ideas on style and layout, and thoroughly check the spelling, first automatically and then by hand. The text warns that even professional checkers may miss certain spelling mistakes.
*   **Principle 6: Involve Your Audience:** The documents produced often become less important than the process used to create them. If possible, the individual should involve the readers by getting their feedback and asking for their input on early drafts of a document. This process builds a good working relationship and usually leads to a better document.
*   **Principle 7: Be a Listener:** One technique mandatory for gaining people's attention is listening to them. Even in a formal meeting where the individual possesses all the information and stands in front of 20 suits, if the individual does not listen to others, the others will not listen to the individual. To encourage people to speak, one should ask questions or ask the people to restate the discussion in their own words. Transforming the meeting into a dialogue helps the individual make the point more effectively, and the individual might even learn something in the process.
*   **Principle 8: Get Back to People:** If an individual asks someone a question, the person may feel impolite if they do not respond. Similarly, individuals often fail to respond to emails or memos requesting information or action. In the rush of daily life, it is easy to forget. One must always respond to emails and voicemails, even if the response is simply, “I’ll get back to you later.” Keeping people informed makes them more forgiving of occasional errors and helps them feel that they have not been forgotten.
