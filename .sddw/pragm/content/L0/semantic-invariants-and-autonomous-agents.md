# semantic-invariants-and-autonomous-agents

**Parent:** [[content/L1/software-design-principles-and-process|software-design-principles-and-process]] — Software development requires adherence to principles like DRY, which mandates single, authoritative knowledge representations, and employing techniques such as PERT (optimistic, most likely, pessimistic times) and 'Eating the Elephant' (iterative thin slices) to manage uncertainty.

Semantic invariants describe inviolate requirements, functioning as a fundamental "philosophical contract." For example, when developing a debit card transaction switch, a critical requirement was that the user of the debit card must never have the same transaction applied to the account twice. Regardless of the type of failure mode, the system's error should focus on failing to process a transaction rather than processing a duplicate transaction. This simple law, derived directly from the project's requirements, proved highly effective in resolving complex error recovery scenarios and guided detailed design and implementation in many areas.

Crucially, the reader must distinguish between requirements that are fixed, inviolate laws (semantic invariants) and those that are merely policies subject to change due to a new management regime. A semantic invariant must be central to the very meaning of something and cannot be subject to the whims of policy, which is reserved for dynamic business rules.

When a requirement qualifies as a semantic invariant, the developer must ensure it becomes a well-known part of the system documentation, whether that documentation is a bulleted list in a signed requirements document or a simple note on a common whiteboard visible to everyone. The invariant should be stated clearly and unambiguously. For instance, concerning the debit card example, the statement "Err in favor of the consumer" serves as a clear, concise, unambiguous contract with all users of the system and guarantees the system's behavior.

While the preceding discussion focused on contracts as fixed, immutable specifications, the landscape of autonomous agents changes this requirement. By definition, autonomous agents are free to reject requests they do not want to honor. These agents also have the freedom to renegotiate the contract—for example, proposing, "I cannot provide that, but if you give me this, I might provide something else." Any system that relies on agent technology has a critical dependence on contractual arrangements, even if those contracts are generated dynamically. If developers cannot utilize contracts manually, they will not be able to implement them automatically. Therefore, when designing any piece of software, the developer must design the system's contract as well.
