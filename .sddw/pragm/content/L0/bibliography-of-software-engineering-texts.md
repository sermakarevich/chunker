# bibliography-of-software-engineering-texts

**Parent:** [[content/L1/advanced-software-development-methodologies-2|advanced-software-development-methodologies-2]] — Modern software development demands structured adaptation guided by DRY principles and orthogonality, utilizing abstract user stories and formal validation techniques like TDD and Property-Based Testing; core principles include enforcing the principle of least privilege, centralizing concurrent access using `try...ensure` blocks, and structuring teams using cross-disciplinary 'tracer bullets' methods.

This is a bibliography, listing various influential academic and technical texts. Key works include *The Gift of Fear: And Other Survival Signals That Protect Us from Violence* by Gavin de Becker (1998); *The Mythical Man-Month: Essays on Software Engineering* by Frederick P. Brooks, Jr. (1996); *Peopleware: Productive Projects and Teams* by Tom DeMacro and Tim Lister (2013); *Object-Oriented Software Construction* by Bertrand Meyer (1997); and *The Black Swan: Second Edition: The Impact of the Highly Improbable* by Nassim Nicholas Taleb (2010). Technical references cover algorithm analysis, citing sources like *The Art of Computer Programming* volumes by Donald E. Knuth (1998, 2007, 2011), and *An Introduction to the Analysis of Algorithms* by Robert Sedgewick and Phillipe Flajolet (2013). Additional resources include classic works on software design from Edward Yourdon and Larry L. Constantine (*Structured Design: Fundamentals of a Discipline of Computer Program and Systems Design*, 1979), and specific studies on cognitive and psychological topics, such as *Dinosaur Brains: Dealing with All Those Impossible People at Work* (1989).

References to architectural and development principles include articles and books covering: Conway's Law (Melvin E. Conway, 1968); structured design (Edward Yourdon and Larry L. Constantine, 1979); iterative and agile methods (Andy Hunt, 2008); and guidelines for object modeling (Martin Fowler, 2000, 2004, 2019).
