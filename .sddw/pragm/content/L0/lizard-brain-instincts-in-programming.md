Gavin de Becker’s life’s work is helping people to protect themselves from violence, which is summarized in his book, *The Gift of Fear: And Other Survival Signals*. One of the core themes presented in the book is that, even for sophisticated humans, our instincts—our lizard brain—are critical. De Becker claims that most people who are attacked in the street are actually aware of feeling uncomfortable or nervous *before* the attack occurs, though people often tell themselves that this feeling is silly. Instincts are simply responses to patterns stored in the nonconscious brain, which can be innate or learned through repetition. As a programmer gains experience, the brain constantly lays down layers of tacit knowledge, including the probable causes of specific errors and general operational principles. This knowledge storage process happens even when the programmer is simply talking to someone, without the programmer realizing that the activity is causing the learning. Regardless of their source, all instincts share the characteristic of having no words; instincts make a person feel, not think. Therefore, when an instinct is triggered, the developer does not see a flashing lightbulb with a banner wrapped around it; instead, the developer might get nervous, queasy, or feel that the work is overwhelming. The strategy involves first noticing that the instinctive feeling is happening, and then working to understand the cause. Two common situations where the inner lizard might offer a signal are discussed: facing the blank page and feeling resistance, and experiencing difficulty while coding. For the first situation, many developers feel intense apprehension when starting a new project or even a new module in an existing project. The author proposes that the nagging doubt or reluctance a developer feels when facing a task may be the accumulated experience trying to communicate with the developer. While the developer may not know the exact source of the problem, giving the doubt time will likely make the concern clearer and more solvable. The second problem, which is more practical, is the developer's fear of making a mistake. Because developers invest a significant amount of themselves into their code, they often treat errors in the code as reflections on their own competence, sometimes manifesting as imposter syndrome, leading the developer to believe the current project is beyond the developer's capability. When coding difficulty increases, the code itself signals this stress. The code suggests that the current situation is harder than it should be, possibly indicating that the structure or design is flawed, the wrong problem is being solved, or that the developer is simply creating an excessive number of bugs. The developer's lizard brain is sensing this feedback from the code and desperately urges the developer to pay attention. To actively listen to the inner lizard brain, the developer should first stop coding and allow the brain time and space to organize itself. Techniques include taking a walk, having lunch, chatting with someone, or simply sleeping on the issue. If those methods fail, the developer should try externalizing the issue by creating doodles about the code, explaining the problem to a coworker (especially one who is not a programmer), or talking to a rubber duck. If the problem persists after these efforts, the developer should resort to prototyping. To start a prototype, the developer should look for an aspect of the project that the developer wants to explore, such as testing a new framework's data binding capability or examining an algorithm on specific edge cases. If the developer is working on existing code that resists change, the developer should stash the existing code and prototype something similar instead.
