# coding-process-critical-thinking

**Parent:** [[content/L1/concurrency-architectural-patterns-and-coding-practices|concurrency-architectural-patterns-and-coding-practices]] — Software development requires continuous, non-mechanical critical decision-making, underpinned by architectural principles like DRY, orthogonality, and decoupling; for concurrency, the Actor Model (featuring independent state, mailboxes, and message-only communication) and Blackboard Systems (allowing asynchronous coordination by independent agents) are preferred alternatives to traditional shared resources, even if they increase system complexity.

Chapter 7: While You Are Coding. Conventional wisdom incorrectly suggests that once a project enters the coding phase, the work becomes mechanical, merely transcribing the design into executable statements. This limited view is considered the single biggest reason that software projects fail, often resulting in systems that are ugly, inefficient, poorly structured, unmaintainable, or incorrect. Coding is not mechanical; continuous decision-making requires careful thought and judgment to ensure that the resulting program remains accurate, productive, and functional over time. Even decisions that are not consciously made can be harnessed by actively listening to instincts and nonconscious thoughts (detailed in Topic 37, Listen to Your Lizard Brain). However, developers who do not actively think about their code are programming by coincidence—meaning the code may function, but for no specific reason. The author advocates for a more positive involvement with the coding process (Topic 38, Programming by Coincidence). Regarding performance, while most of the code written executes quickly, developers sometimes develop algorithms with the potential to bog down even the fastest processors. Topic 39, Algorithm Speed, discusses methods to estimate code speed and provides tips for spotting potential problems before they occur. Pragmatic Programmers must think critically about all code, including their own, and constantly identify opportunities for improvement in their programs and designs (Topic 40, Refactoring). Furthermore, testing is not solely about finding bugs; testing provides crucial feedback on the code's design, API, and coupling. Therefore, the major benefits of testing accrue when the developer thinks about and writes the tests, not just when running them (Topic 41, Test to Code). Developers must also acknowledge their own biases when testing their code (Topic 42, Property-Based Testing). Critically, the resulting code must be readable and easy to reason about because the real world is filled with bad actors who attempt to breach the system and cause harm. The text will discuss basic techniques and approaches to help developers stay secure (Topic 43, Stay Safe Out There). Finally, the process of naming things (Topic 44, Naming Things) is challenging, as the names chosen often define the reality of the created system. Throughout the entire coding process, developers must remain aware of any potential semantic drift. The analogy of driving a car illustrates this principle: although driving can be routine, good, safe drivers constantly review the situation, check for potential problems, and proactively position themselves to handle unexpected events. Similarly, while coding may be routine, keeping constant awareness is necessary to prevent a disaster.
