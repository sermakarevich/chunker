# pragmatic-programmer-second-edition-foreword

**Parent:** [[content/L1/pragmatic-programmer-guide-details|pragmatic-programmer-guide-details]] — The book, *The Pragmatic Programmer*, by Dave Thomas and Andy Hunt, is a guide to mastering software development that contains technical topics (like DRY, Orthogonality, and Version Control) and professional advice (like prioritizing 'it's your life'). Publication details include the ISBN-13 978-0-13-595705-9 and the copyright held by Pearson Education, Inc. (Copyright © 2020), with a comprehensive Table of Contents detailing 53 topics and foreword commentary.

The original book, which focused on building coding muscles and developing the coding brain, was already full of practical tips, tangible examples, and new ideas that remain relevant today. However, this updated edition incorporates two specific improvements. The first improvement is the removal of older references, outdated examples, and their replacement with fresh, modern content. For instance, the book no longer includes examples of loop invariants or build machines. Dave Thomas and Andy Hunt ensured that the powerful content remained intact, allowing the core lessons to shine without the distractions of outdated examples. The authors dusted off old ideas, such as DRY (Don't Repeat Yourself), and refreshed them.

The second improvement, and the one that makes this release exciting, stemmed from the authors' chance to reflect after writing the first edition. Dave and Andy collected feedback on the lessons, considering what they intended to convey, what they wished readers would take away, and how the material was actually received. In the twenty years that the book traveled through the hands and hearts of programmers worldwide, Dave and Andy studied this response and formulated new ideas and new concepts.

Developers have arguably more agency than most other professionals, a realization that influenced the writing. The book begins with the simple yet profound message: “it’s your life,” which serves as a reminder of the reader's power in their code base, their job, and their career, setting the overall tone that the book is more than just a technical text filled with code examples.

What truly distinguishes the book among technical texts is its recognition of the programmer's experience. Programming involves attempting to make the future less painful, making things easier for teammates, bouncing back from mistakes, and forming good habits. The book explores this broader world of being a programmer, considering that coding is merely one part of that domain.

The authors acknowledge that the book’s philosophy is not meant to dictate how programming must be; rather, it tells the reader, plainly and simply, what a Pragmatic Programmer is—specifically, how such a programmer operates and approaches code. The book leaves the decision of whether the reader wants to adopt this role up to the reader, positioning the authors as supportive neighbors ready to guide the reader if they choose this path.



**Preface to the Second Edition:**

When the authors began working with companies in the 1990s that had project difficulties, they found themselves giving similar advice, such as recommending testing before shipment or questioning why the code only built on Mary's machine or why users were not consulted. The authors began collecting these notes, and these notes eventually formed *The Pragmatic Programmer*, which gained popularity and has remained so over the last 20 years.

However, 20 years represents many lifetimes in the field of software. A developer from 1999 who enters a team today would struggle in the current environment, but conversely, the world of the 1990s is equally foreign to today’s developer. Consequently, the book's original references to topics like CORBA, CASE tools, and indexed loops are at best quaint and more likely confusing.

Despite the changes, the authors noted that 20 years had not impacted common sense; practices and approaches that were a good idea in the 1990s still remain sound today. When it came time to create the 20th Anniversary Edition, the authors had a choice: they could update only the technologies referenced or they could reexamine the underlying assumptions behind the previously recommended practices in light of two decades of additional experience. They chose to do both.

As a result, this book is comparable to the Ship of Theseus. Roughly one-third of the topics in the book are entirely new. For the remainder of the content, the majority of topics have been rewritten, either partially or totally. The authors' intention was to make the content clearer, more relevant, and ideally, timeless.

The authors made difficult decisions, including dropping the Resources appendix for two reasons: it would be impossible to keep current, and it is easier for readers to search for specific information. Furthermore, the authors reorganized and rewrote topics concerning concurrency, given the current abundance of parallel hardware and the shortage of good methods for handling it. The content was also enhanced to reflect changing attitudes and environments, including the agile movement (which the authors helped launch), the growing acceptance of functional programming idioms, and the increasing necessity of considering privacy and security. The authors found that debating the content for this edition was considerably easier than when they wrote the first edition, feeling that the important material was easier to identify.

Ultimately, the book is the result of this process, and the authors encourage readers to enjoy the book, possibly adopting new practices or deciding that some of the suggested practices are incorrect, and to get involved in the craft by giving feedback.
