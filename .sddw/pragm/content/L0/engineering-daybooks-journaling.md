# engineering-daybooks-journaling

**Parent:** [[content/L1/advanced-software-development-methodologies|advanced-software-development-methodologies]] — Professional development necessitates adopting principles like DRY, Design by Contract (DBC) which formalizes function expectations into Preconditions, Postconditions, and Class Invariants, and continuous process refinement using methods like PERT and the Tracer Bullet approach.

Engineering daybooks, or journals, are a technique used in professional settings, initially observed by the author when the author's colleague, Dave, worked with electronic and mechanical engineers at a small computer manufacturer. These engineers used physical paper notebooks, which the engineers would open and write in to record everything related to their work. These daily journals served as a centralized repository for recordings, including what the engineers accomplished, new things the engineers learned, sketches of ideas, and readings from meters.

**Function and Benefits of Daybooks**
Authors use daybooks for practical purposes, such as taking notes during meetings, jotting down current project work, recording variable values while debugging, leaving physical reminders, documenting wild ideas, and occasionally doodling.

The daybook provides three main benefits:

1. **Reliability over Memory:** The daybook is a more reliable tool than human memory. For instance, if someone asks a person about a company called the power supply problem that the person called the previous week, the daybook allows the user to quickly find the name and number.
2. **Idea Storage:** The daybook provides a physical place to store ideas that are not immediately relevant to the task at hand. This allows the writer to maintain concentration on the current task, knowing that the great idea will not be forgotten.
3. **Reflection (Rubber Ducking):** The physical act of writing something down helps the brain switch gears, acting almost like talking to someone. This provides an opportunity for reflection; the writer may begin a note and then suddenly realize that the topic of the note is incorrect.

Authors suggest that professionals try keeping an engineering daybook using paper, rather than a file or a wiki, because the act of writing is unique compared to typing. While the author notes that the daybook's original use was for recording work details, it also offers the added benefit of allowing the user to reflect on people, projects, and clothing/hairstyles from many years ago.
