# collaboration-and-conways-law

When designing software, developers should first consider how the team's internal communication structures will constrain the resulting system architecture, a concept formalized by Conway's Law (Melvin Conway, 1967). This law states that organizations designing systems are constrained to produce designs that are copies of the communication structures of the organization. Consequently, the social structures and communication pathways of the development team and the larger organization will be mirrored in the resulting application, website, or product.

Historical studies support this observation, showing, for example, that teams where team members have no communication pathways among them tend to create siloed, "stove-pipe" systems. Conversely, teams split into two groups often result in clear client/server or frontend/backend architectural divisions. Furthermore, studies suggest that deliberately structuring the development team can influence the code architecture; for instance, geographically distributed teams tend to produce more modular, distributed software. Crucially, development teams that include users in the process will produce software that clearly reflects that involvement, while teams that exclude users will reflect that exclusion.

**Pair Programming**
Pair programming, an eXtreme Programming practice, involves two developers working together on a single problem: one developer operates the keyboard (the typist), while the second developer considers the problem. The developers can switch typing duties as needed. This process offers multiple benefits. Different developers bring varied backgrounds, problem-solving techniques, approaches, and levels of focus to the task. The developer acting as the typist focuses on the low-level details of syntax and coding style, while the second developer is free to consider higher-level concerns and the scope. A second developer’s full brain available during the task provides significantly more mental power than the typist's alone, which is helpful because human cognitive bandwidth is limited when dealing with technical constraints.

The inherent peer pressure from a second developer helps prevent moments of weakness and bad habits, such as naming variables generically (e.g., `foo`). The presence of an actively observing second developer increases the quality of the resulting software by making the developer less inclined to take embarrassing shortcuts.

**Mob Programming**
Mob programming is an extension of pair programming involving more than two developers working on the same problem simultaneously, with typically only one typist. Proponents report positive results using mobs to solve complex problems. Mobs can easily include individuals not conventionally considered part of the core development team, such as users, project sponsors, and testers. In one instance, a small mob of three included one typist and two others who discussed the issue with a business expert. 

**Adopting Collaborative Practices**
If an individual developer currently works alone, the developer should try pair programming, committing to a minimum of two weeks, even if only for a few hours at a time, because the process may feel strange initially. For brainstorming new ideas or diagnosing difficult issues, attempting a mob programming session is suggested. When already pairing or mobbing, the team must determine who is included: Is participation limited to developers, or can the team include extended members such as users, testers, and sponsors? 

As with all collaborative efforts, the team must manage both the technical and human aspects. Tips for getting started include: building the code, not the individual's ego; starting small, perhaps with a mob of only 4-5 people or beginning with a few pairs in short sessions; criticizing the code rather than the person (e.g., saying “Let’s look at this block” instead of “you’re wrong”); listening actively to and seeking to understand others’ viewpoints, recognizing that different views are not inherently wrong; and conducting frequent retrospectives to facilitate improvement for future work. 

Regardless of the working style—whether in the same office or remotely, alone, in pairs, or in mobs—these methods are effective for solving problems. If the team is accustomed to only one style, the team might want to experiment with a different one. However, before attempting a new style, the team must research and understand the suggested rules and guidelines. It is recommended that the team starts by coding a simple exercise rather than jumping into a complex production project.
