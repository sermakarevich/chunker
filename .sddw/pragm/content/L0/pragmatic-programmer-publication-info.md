She states that the updated content ensures the book will remain at the top of "best books in software development" lists for another 20 years.
*   **Andrea Goulet:** Andrea Goulet, CEO of Corgibytes and Founder of LegacyCode.Rocks, advises readers that if they want their software to be easy to modernize and maintain, they should keep a copy of *The Pragmatic Programmer* close, as it is filled with practical advice, both technical and professional, that will serve the reader and their projects for years to come.
*   **Obie Fernandez:** Obie Fernandez, author of *The Rails Way*, stated that *The Pragmatic Programmer* was a seminal work that completely altered his career trajectory in software, leading him to view himself as a craftsman rather than just a cog in a big machine. He considers it one of the most significant books in his life.
*   **David A. Black:** David A. Black, author of *The Well-Grounded Rubyist*, notes that first-time readers can expect an engaging introduction to the modern world of software practice, which the first edition largely shaped. Readers who have read the first edition will rediscover updated and expertly curated insights and practical wisdom. He also mentioned that while the book is read on an iPad and the code examples use modern programming languages, the underlying concepts, ideas, and attitudes remain timeless and universally applicable, suggesting the book remains relevant twenty years later.
*   **Sandy Mamoli:** Sandy Mamoli, Agile coach and author of *How Self-Selection Lets People Excel*, states that the first edition of *The Pragmatic Programmer* changed his career trajectory twenty years ago, and the new edition could do the same for the reader.

**Foreword:**

In the Foreword, the author recounts watching the coding community react with excitement to the news of the second edition of *The Pragmatic Programmer* twenty years later. The author, who had the privilege of reading an unreleased copy, observes that calling the book merely a 'technical book' does it a disservice, as technical books often intimidate readers with big words, obscure terms, and convoluted examples. The author praises Dave Thomas and Andy Hunt for conquering the difficult challenge of writing with the same excitement of people who are just learning the lessons. The authors do not talk down to the reader, assume the reader is an expert, or even assume the reader has read the first edition; instead, they treat all readers as programmers who merely want to get better and provide actionable steps throughout the book.
