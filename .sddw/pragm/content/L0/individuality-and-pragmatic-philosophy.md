# individuality-and-pragmatic-philosophy

**Parent:** [[content/L1/pragmatic-programmer-philosophy-and-craftsmanship|pragmatic-programmer-philosophy-and-craftsmanship]] — The book defines a Pragmatic Programmer as an attitude that requires considering all problems within their larger context, emphasizing continuous, small improvements (*Kaizen*) and asserting that individual craftsmanship is vital even in large engineering projects. It is structured as self-contained topics that readers can follow in any order and includes exercises, challenges, and a detailed appendix.

Regarding individual craftsmanship on large teams and complex projects, some people assert that individual members must not make independent decisions, stating that "Software is an engineering discipline" that breaks down if individuals make unauthorized decisions. However, the book strongly disagrees with this assertion, maintaining that even if engineering is required in software construction, this does not preclude individual craftsmanship. For example, the large cathedrals built in Europe during the Middle Ages, which required thousands of person-years of effort spread over many decades, relied on skilled craftspeople—including carpenters, stonecutters, carvers, and glass workers—who interpreted the general engineering requirements to produce a cohesive whole that went beyond purely mechanical construction. These craftspeople sustained the projects through their belief in individual contributions: "We who cut mere stones must always be envisioning cathedrals." Within the overall structure of any project, there is always room for individuality and craftsmanship, a concept particularly relevant to the current state of software engineering. The current engineering practices may seem archaic one hundred years from now, much like medieval cathedral techniques appear to today’s civil engineers, but the accompanying craftsmanship will remain honored.

Separately, a passage describes the concept of continuous improvement, using the analogy of a lawn. A tourist asked a gardener how the lawn remained so perfect, and the gardener replied that the secret was simple: brushing off dew every morning, mowing every other day, and rolling once a week. When the tourist asked if that was all, the gardener replied, "Absolutely," suggesting that repeating these efforts for 500 years would yield a nice lawn. Similarly, great programmers require small amounts of daily care. The book introduces *Kaizen*, a Japanese term representing the concept of continuously making small improvements. This principle was historically considered a primary reason for the dramatic gains in productivity and quality in Japanese manufacturing and was widely adopted globally. *Kaizen* applies to personal skill development as well; the advice is to work daily to refine existing skills and add new tools to one's repertoire. Unlike the Eton lawns analogy, the book suggests that professional improvements will yield visible results within days, allowing experience and skills to blossom and grow over time.

Finally, in a section labeled 'A Pragmatic Philosophy,' the text states that the book is about the reader's career, specifically titled 'Topic 1. It’s Your Life.' The text advises readers that they own their career and are reading the book because they know they can improve their skills and help others do the same, allowing them to become a Pragmatic Programmer. The distinguishing trait of Pragmatic Programmers is viewed as an attitude, a style, or a philosophy for approaching problems and their solutions. These programmers consider beyond the immediate problem, placing it within its larger context and searching for the bigger picture, which is necessary for making informed decisions and intelligent compromises. Another key trait is that Pragmatic Programmers take responsibility for everything they do. The text further advises that, in 'Topic 2. The Cat Ate My Source Code,' the book discusses the responsibility of Pragmatic Programmers, noting that responsible programmers will not watch their projects decline due to neglect. In 'Topic 3. Software Entropy,' the book instructs readers on how to keep their projects maintained.
