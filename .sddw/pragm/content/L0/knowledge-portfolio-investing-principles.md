Topic 6: Your Knowledge Portfolio

(Opening quote by Benjamin Franklin: "An investment in knowledge always pays the best interest.")

In the professional development sphere, knowledge and experience are the most crucial day-to-day assets for programmers. However, these assets are considered expiring assets because knowledge becomes outdated as new techniques, languages, and environments are developed. Since the pace of change in technology is ever-increasing, professional experience may quickly become obsolete or irrelevant. As the value of a programmer's knowledge declines, the value to the company or client also decreases, necessitating efforts to prevent this decline.

Therefore, the most important strategic asset for a programmer is the ability to learn new things. A key question is: how can one learn how to learn, and what subjects should one study? To manage a knowledge portfolio, the text suggests viewing all facts programmers know about computing, the application domains they work in, and their accumulated experience as a 'knowledge portfolio.' Managing this portfolio closely mirrors the process of managing a financial portfolio. Successful investment in a knowledge portfolio requires following these guidelines:

1. **Invest Regularly:** Knowledge must be invested in consistently, even if the amount is small. The habit of continuous investment is as vital as the specific amount of knowledge gained. Therefore, one should plan to dedicate a consistent time and place for this activity, free from interruptions.
2. **Diversification:** Knowing a wide variety of subjects makes the programmer more valuable. While the programmer must understand the intricacies of the current technology they are using, they should not stop there. Because the face of computing changes rapidly, hot technology today might be useless or undesirable tomorrow. Expanding comfort across multiple technologies, including nontechnical areas, enhances the programmer's ability to adapt to change.
3. **Manage Risk:** Regarding technology, the options exist on a spectrum, ranging from low-risk/low-reward to high-risk/high-reward. Programmers should avoid committing all their efforts to high-risk technologies that might suddenly fail, nor should they remain exclusively with conservative options and miss out on potential opportunities. The guidance is to avoid concentrating all technical efforts in one area.
4. **Buy Low, Sell High:** Learning an emerging technology before it gains popularity can be highly rewarding, similar to finding an undervalued stock. For example, learning Java when it was first introduced and unknown may have been initially risky, but it paid significant dividends for early adopters when the language became an industry standard.
5. **Review and Rebalance:** Since the industry is dynamic, technologies can become outdated rapidly. This guideline suggests reviewing existing knowledge—such as brushing up on a database technology that hasn't been used in a while—or adjusting focus to better prepare for a new job opening by trying out a different language.

Of these guidelines, the most important one and the easiest to implement is the commitment to **Invest Regularly in Your Knowledge Portfolio**.

**Goals for Acquiring Intellectual Capital:**

To fund the knowledge portfolio, programmers should consider several methods:

*   **Learn at least one new language every year:** Learning various programming languages is beneficial because different languages can solve the same problems using different methods. This approach helps broaden thinking and prevents stagnation. Learning many languages is made easier by the abundance of freely available software.
*   **Read a technical book each month:** While online resources provide many short-form essays and sometimes reliable answers, deep understanding requires long-form books. Programmers should browse booksellers for technical books on topics related to their current projects. Once a habit is established, the goal is to read a book monthly; after mastering current technologies, readers should branch out to study subjects unrelated to their immediate projects.
